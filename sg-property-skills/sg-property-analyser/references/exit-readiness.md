# Exit Readiness Framework

## Overview: Why Plan Your Exit Before You Buy

The difference between a successful property investment and a painful forced sale is **exit readiness**. Exit readiness is the discipline of evaluating a property not just for its own merits (cash flow, yield, location charm), but specifically for how easily and profitably you can exit when you choose to, under stress, or when circumstances force a sale.

Many property investors buy optimistically ("This location will transform!"), hold through market cycles, and then discover at exit time that the buyer pool is too narrow, the lease decay matters more than appreciated, or the unit mix they chose appeals only to a microscopic segment. By then, it's too late.

This framework structures exit readiness into **6 categories** and **10 scored dimensions**, so that you can evaluate projects systematically before you buy.

---

## The 6-Category Exit Readiness Framework

### Category 1: Entry Price Assessment

**Why it matters for exit:** The price you pay is the floor for all exit scenarios. Too high an entry price means you need exceptional appreciation just to break even. Too low, and you might lack buyer pool appeal (overpriced units are easy to buy, cheap units are easy to sell, but "too cheap" signals quality issues).

**Sub-factors:**
- **Quantum Accessibility** (MOAT Dimension: Quantum Effect)
  - What % of Singapore households can afford this exit price at Year 5?
  - Score 1–5: 5 = <$1.2M (35% of households), 3 = $1.5–1.8M, 1 = >$2.5M (5% only)
  - Why: A unit that requires $1.8M buyer pool has 10× fewer buyers than a $1.2M unit

- **Lease Decay Risk** (MOAT Dimension: Bala's Curve Effect)
  - For freehold: score 5 (no decay)
  - For leasehold: score 1–5 based on remaining lease at exit
    - ≥80yr remaining = 5 (strong valuation)
    - 70–79yr = 4
    - 60–69yr = 3 (critical threshold: some banks hesitant)
    - 50–59yr = 2
    - <50yr = 1 (severely discounted)
  - Why: A 75-year lease at purchase becomes 70-year at Year 5 exit. Banks tighten, buyers demand heavy discount.

- **New-Launch vs Resale Gap** (QPE Framework principle)
  - Research: what's the price gap between new launches and comparable 3–5yr-old resale units in the same area?
    - Narrow gap (0–15%) → favour new launch (you exit as a fresher condo)
    - Wide gap (45–70%) → favour cheapest resale (the price delta is too large to overcome)
  - Why: The gap represents market's forward price discovery. A shrinking gap is your tail wind; a widening gap is your headwind.

### Category 2: Location Features

**Why it matters for exit:** Location determines who your buyer pool is. An MRT-adjacent unit with top-tier schools appeals to families; a peripheral condo with weak schools appeals to investors only. The narrower the buyer pool, the harder the exit.

**Sub-factors:**
- **MRT Proximity Band** (MOAT Dimension: MRT Proximity)
  - <100m sheltered = 5 (highest liquidity)
  - <350m walk = 4
  - <500m walk = 3
  - <1km = 2
  - >1km = 1 (severely limits buyer pool)
  - Why: Buyers under-utilise search radius beyond 1km MRT. Below this threshold, average Days-on-Market increases exponentially.

- **School Tier Premium** (MOAT Dimension: Parents' Attraction Effect)
  - Top 20 primary schools within 1km = 5 (family demand multiplier)
  - Top 50 = 4
  - Top 100 = 3
  - No schools within 1km = 1
  - Why: Families with young children represent your safest exit pool. Their demand is inelastic (they won't move districts for a 5% price cut).

- **Future Catalysts** (Qualitative signal, not scored)
  - GLS land sales, park connector openings, integrated developments, area transformation plans
  - How to gather: URA live list, ST property supplements, CCB announcements
  - Exit impact: Catalysts widen the buyer pool 3–5 years out. Early catalyst + exit timing = significant uplift.

### Category 3: Location Profile

**Why it matters for exit:** A property's context (the cluster, the district, the region) determines valuation ceiling and buyer affordability caps.

**Sub-factors:**
- **District Median Income Tier** (MOAT Dimension: District Disparity Effect)
  - Quintile 5 (CCR, Mattar, Bukit Timah): District median HHI $25K+/mo → buyers with highest affordability
  - Quintile 4 (Sengkang, Tampines, Clementi): District median $18–22K/mo → strong mid-market demand
  - Quintile 3 (Bedok, Pasir Ris, Yio Chu Kang): District median $14–17K/mo → solid family pool
  - Quintile 2–1 (Choa Chu Kang, Woodlands, Bukit Batok): District median <$14K/mo → tight affordability at purchase, tight at exit
  - Why: If you price above the district median HHI, you've segmented yourself to outsiders (investors, foreigners, internal transfers). This shrinks the buyer pool materially.

- **Cluster Type & Density** (Qualitative signal, not scored)
  - HDB cluster: Attracts HDB upgraders (predictable, loyal buyer pool, but susceptible to HDB price moves)
  - Condo cluster: Mixed investor + own-stay, broad buyer base
  - Landed cluster: Narrow pool (owner-occupiers only), but sticky demand
  - Why: HDB clusters have less liquidity but high certainty of HDB upgraders. Condo clusters have broad but volatile demand.

- **Region Classification (CCR/RCR/OCR)** (MOAT Dimension: Region Disparity Effect)
  - CCR (Central: D9/10/11): Premium, max 2% annual appreciation headroom built into pricing, but deep buyer pool
  - RCR (Rest of Central: D1–4/7/8/12–15): Growth zone, wider appreciation scenarios, strong liquidity
  - OCR-fringe (Punggol, Jurong, Sengkang emerging): Growth + discovery, liquidity varies
  - OCR-deep (Lim Chu Kang, Changi, Bukit Panjang edges): Speculative, thin exit pool, high hold-period risk
  - Why: RCR/OCR-fringe offer best exit optionality (price upside + broad buyer interest). CCR is safe but expensive. OCR-deep is risky.

### Category 4: Project Features

**Why it matters for exit:** The project's physical product, scale, and uniqueness determine how many units compete for your buyer at exit.

**Sub-factors:**
- **Site Density** (MOAT Dimension: Landsize Density Effect)
  - <60 units/hectare = 5 (boutique, low competition, strong scarcity premium)
  - 60–120 units/ha = 4 (small/medium, balanced)
  - 120–200 units/ha = 3 (medium, moderate competition)
  - >200 units/ha = 2 (mega, commoditised)
  - Why: Buyers remember projects with <100 units (scarcity perception). >500-unit projects blur into "just another condo" at resale.

- **Unit Type Breadth & Mix** (Qualitative signal, not scored)
  - If you buy a 2BR in a 1BR–5BR mix: strong buyer pool (investors cover 1–2BR, families cover 3–5BR)
  - If you buy a 1BR in a 3BR–4BR–5BR project: narrow pool (investors only, families avoid small)
  - Why: Mixed-type projects have dual buyer appeal. Single-type projects force you to compete within a narrow segment.

- **Project USPs** (Qualitative signal, not scored)
  - Freehold tenure = +3–5% valuation premium at exit, attracts long-term hold buyers
  - Integrated development (mall, MRT direct, offices) = liquidity multiplier (new buyer segments emerge)
  - Waterfront / river-facing / views = emotional premium, but volatile (buyers who pay for views are speculative)
  - Why: USPs broaden the buyer pool (emotional buyers) but also increase volatility (emotion ≠ stability).

### Category 5: Supply and Demand

**Why it matters for exit:** Supply scarcity and demand depth determine your negotiating position at exit. Abundant supply + weak demand = forced concession. Tight supply + strong demand = you set the price.

**Sub-factors:**
- **Transaction Liquidity / Volume Effect** (MOAT Dimension: Volume Effect)
  - >120 deals/yr = 5 (extremely liquid, <30 days average time-on-market)
  - 60–120 deals/yr = 4 (liquid)
  - 30–60 deals/yr = 3 (normal)
  - 10–30 deals/yr = 2 (illiquid)
  - <10 deals/yr = 1 (distressed exit risk)
  - Why: Transaction volume is the best proxy for exit speed. High volume = certainty of exit. Low volume = extended holding period at risk.

- **Within-Project Competition** (Qualitative signal, not scored)
  - How many units of your type (2BR, 3BR, etc.) are listed for resale at any moment?
  - <3 units listed = scarcity, buyer has to wait
  - 3–10 units listed = normal, moderate negotiation
  - >10 units listed = competitive, you compete on price/condition
  - Why: If 5 other 2BR units are listed at the same price, you're a commodity.

- **Rental Demand Strength** (MOAT Dimension: Rental Demand)
  - Score 1–5: Assess the rental tenant pool and rental yield relative to district average
  - Why: If you can't sell, can you hold and rent? Weak rental demand = you must sell, even at a loss.

---

### Category 6: Exit Buyer Profiles

**Why it matters for exit:** Different buyer types have different decision triggers and price sensitivity. Knowing who your exit buyers are, and how many of them exist, is the ultimate exit readiness metric.

**Sub-factors:**
- **Exit Audience Width** (MOAT Dimension: Exit Audience)
  - Score 1–5 based on breadth:
    - 5 = 3+ buyer profiles interested (families, investors, upgraders all compete)
    - 3 = 2 buyer profiles (e.g., investors + young couples)
    - 1 = 1 buyer profile only (e.g., investors only; families avoid)
  - Why: The more buyer profiles, the more resilient your exit in downturns. A unit that appeals to families AND investors has 3× the buyer pool.

- **The 5 Exit Buyer Categories** (Qualitative, use to assess breadth)
  1. **First-time Buyers** (younger, lower affordability ceiling ~$1.2–1.4M, MRT-dependent)
  2. **HDB Upgraders** (upgrading from HDB, affordability $1.3–1.8M, family-focused, schools matter)
  3. **Families with Young Children** (upgrading for space, school quality critical, MRT less important than schools)
  4. **Retirees Downgrading** (from larger to smaller/lower-cost, affordability varies, prefer mature/safe areas)
  5. **Property Investors** (yield-driven, MRT/location less important, ROI thresholds matter, rental demand critical)

- **Post-COVID Buyer Preference Shift** (Market context, not scored)
  - 3BR+ units: strong demand increase (work-from-home space, family segregation)
  - 1BR units: demand decline (reduced investor appetite, weak own-stay demand)
  - Implication: If you buy a 1BR now, exit window is narrower post-2025. If you buy 3BR, exit audience broadens.

- **District Appeal Level** (Qualitative signal, not scored)
  - **High-appeal districts** (Bishan, Toa Payoh, Tampines): Draw buyers from across Singapore (not just district residents)
  - **Medium-appeal districts** (Sengkang, Punggol, Bedok): Draw mostly from within district + adjacent
  - **Low-appeal districts** (Choa Chu Kang, Lim Chu Kang, Jurong West): Local buyers only, vulnerable to district-level downturns
  - Why: Buyers in high-appeal districts have more mobility (they'd move district for a good deal). Buyers in low-appeal districts are locked in (forced to stay local).

## RealSmart Pre-Screening Badges (Binary Pass/Fail Gates)

Before you score a project on the 10 MOAT dimensions, apply these 6 pre-screens. A failing badge doesn't disqualify a project, but it flags a material exit headwind.

| Badge | Test | Exit Implication |
|-------|------|-------------------|
| **Under 1km MRT** | mrtDistanceM < 1000 | Pass = broad buyer pool; Fail = narrows buyer pool, especially families |
| **Under 10 years old** | ageYears < 10 | Pass = no lease decay concerns; Fail = accelerated decay risk after Year 5 |
| **Near $1M HDB** | HDB resales >$1M in same/adjacent estate | Pass = HDB upgrader pool exists; Fail = upgrader affordability ceiling may not support your exit price |
| **50+ deals past year** | annualTransactions ≥ 50 | Pass = liquid market; Fail = thin exit pool, extended Days-on-Market |
| **Top 20 schools** | schoolTier === 1 | Pass = family demand premium; Fail = investors only (narrower pool) |
| **HDB Town MOP 1k+** | 1,000+ MOP units in district within 3yr | Pass = HDB upgrader influx; Fail = supply of new HDB may suppress resale prices |

## The Net Proceeds Waterfall: What You Actually Pocket at Exit

When you exit, every dollar of sale price evaporates before it reaches your pocket. Knowing this waterfall in detail prevents the shock of discovering your "$1.8M exit" nets only "$1.4M" after costs.

```
Sale Price (your target)
  − Agent Commission (1% typical, sometimes 2% buyer+seller)
  − SSD (Seller's Stamp Duty): 12% (<12mo), 8% (<24mo), 4% (<36mo), 0% (≥36mo)
  − Legal Fees on Sale (~$1,500)
  ───────────────────────────
  Gross Proceeds (after agent + SSD + legal)
  − Remaining Mortgage Loan
  ───────────────────────────
  After Loan Repayment
  − CPF Refund (principal + 2.5% p.a. accrued interest)
  ───────────────────────────
  NET CASH TO YOU

Example (Base Case, Year 5):
  Sale Price: $1,650K
  Agent Comm (1%): -$16.5K
  SSD (0% after 36mo): $0
  Legal: -$2.5K
  Gross Proceeds: $1,631K
  Remaining Loan: -$892K
  After Loan: $739K
  CPF Refund (principal $80K + interest $10.5K): -$90.5K
  NET: $648.5K ✓
```

## The SSD Cliff: The Most Important Exit Inflection Point

**The SSD cliff is at 36 months.** This is the single most impactful variable in your hold-period decision.

- At 35 months: You owe 4% SSD (e.g., $66K on a $1.65M sale)
- At 37 months: You owe $0 SSD (same $1.65M sale)

This creates a massive IRR delta. An IRR at Month 35 might be 2–4%. At Month 37, the same property at the same price yields 8–10% IRR. **The cliff is real.**

**Strategic implications:**
- If you buy intending to exit Year 2–3: plan for SSD. It's unavoidable.
- If you buy for Year 4–6+: try to hit Year 3+ exit to clear the SSD cliff, or hold past Year 6 (when SSD is stale, appreciation matters more).
- If you buy as a short-flip (<2 years): SSD cost + early exit penalty makes this unprofitable unless appreciation is exceptional.

## Hold Period IRR Sweep: How to Read It and Find Your Optimal Window

The "Hold Period Sweep" table shows you IRR at each hold period (Year 1–7) under your chosen appreciation scenario. Use it to identify:

1. **The SSD cliff** (3yr mark): Look for the IRR jump.
2. **The optimal exit window**: Where does your target IRR get crossed first? That's your minimum hold. Where does it plateau or decline? That's your maximum hold before appreciation headwinds kick in.
3. **The 4–6yr sweet spot**: For most Singapore properties, IRR peaks in Years 4–6 when SSD is cleared and appreciation compounds. Look to exit within this window unless you have strong hold thesis.

Example interpretation:
```
Hold Period Sweep — Base CAGR 4%
─────────────────────────────
Hold    Exit Price    Net Cash    SSD      IRR    Target 10%?
12mo    $1,468K       $398K       $176K    -2.1%    ✗
24mo    $1,514K       $471K       $121K     5.1%    ✗
36mo → SSD CLIFF
37mo    $1,562K       $571K          $0    12.4%    ✓  ← First cross
48mo    $1,612K       $606K          $0    11.2%    ✓
60mo    $1,664K       $643K          $0    10.2%    ✓
72mo    $1,719K       $681K          $0     9.6%    ✗  ← Starting to decline
```

In this example:
- **Minimum hold**: ~37mo (first crosses 10% target, just past SSD cliff)
- **Optimal window**: 37–60mo (meets or exceeds target)
- **Diminishing returns**: Year 6+ (IRR declining, appreciation alone won't save you)

## Required Exit Price Solver: "What Price Do I Need to Hit My Target?"

Instead of asking "what IRR will I get?", reverse it: **"What exit price must I achieve to hit my target 10% IRR?"**

This is the most decision-useful framing because it tells you whether your appreciation assumption is realistic.

Example:
- Your target IRR: 10%
- Your hold period: 5 years
- **Required exit price: $1,672K**
- Implied CAGR needed: 4.2%

Then ask: is 4.2% CAGR realistic for this project over 5 years?
- Base market assumption (4% CAGR): **Just barely clears** the target (tight margin, not safe)
- Bull scenario (6% CAGR): **Clear win** (plenty of upside buffer)
- Bear scenario (2% CAGR): **Misses target** (don't rely on this trade)

**Use this to stress-test your thesis.** If your required price is higher than any realistic scenario, the property doesn't meet your threshold. If it's met by base or bull, you have optionality.

## Summary: Your Exit Readiness Checklist

Before committing capital, score the project against all 6 categories and 10 dimensions. A project scoring <30/50 has material exit headwinds. A project scoring 40+/50 has exit flexibility.

High exit readiness means: **You can exit when you choose, not when you're forced.**
