# Project-Level Analysis Framework

Project quality determines your exit audience and pricing power when you sell. Two units of identical size in same area can have vastly different price appreciation because of project liquidity, amenities, and developer reputation.

## Transaction Volume & Liquidity

A project with strong transaction volume signals healthy price discovery and easy exits. A frozen project (few transactions annually) creates exit risk regardless of area fundamentals.

### How to Interpret Transaction Data

**Data sources:**
- **URA REALIS:** Official transaction database. Subscription required but gold standard. Shows every transaction with price, unit type, floor level, tenure status.
- **Caveats data:** Public record of all property transactions (seller protection to buyer usually hasn't settled). Shows recent sales with address and price.
- **PropertyGuru/99.co transaction history:** Limited free tier; shows recent sales and estimated prices for listed properties.
- **EdgeProp:** Profitability analytics; shows transaction count per project per quarter.

**What constitutes healthy volume:**
- **Medium-sized projects (200-400 units):** 5-8 transactions per quarter = healthy
- **Large projects (400+ units):** 10-15 transactions per quarter = healthy
- **Small projects (<200 units):** 2-3 transactions per quarter = adequate, though proportional to unit count
- **Niche projects (100-150 units):** 1-2 per quarter acceptable if prices are stable

**Warning signs:**
- **Fewer than 1 transaction per quarter:** Illiquid. Extreme difficulty exiting. Valuation uncertainty very high.
- **Declining transaction frequency year-over-year:** Demand deteriorating. Possible structural issue (supply glut, neighbourhood decline, demographic shift).
- **Spikes followed by drought:** Possible distressed sales or refinancing wave, followed by disinterest. Unstable market.

### Why Volume Matters: Bid-Ask Spread Mechanics

**In high-volume projects:**
- Many buyers actively looking → sellers have leverage to price appropriately
- Price discovery is efficient → asking prices and transaction prices track closely
- Exit timeline: 1-3 months typical on market before sale
- Bid-ask spread: 2-5% typical (seller and buyer not far apart on value)

**In low-volume projects:**
- Few buyers actively searching → seller must overprice to attract any interest or underprice to guarantee sale
- Price discovery is poor → asking prices and transaction prices may diverge significantly
- Exit timeline: 4-8 months on market or longer; may require price cuts to move
- Bid-ask spread: 8-15% typical (uncertainty about "fair value" widens spread)

**Example:** Same 3-bedroom unit in Tiong Bahru (high-volume, 8 transactions/quarter) vs Tanglin (medium-volume, 2-3 transactions/quarter):
- Tiong Bahru unit list at $1.5M → receives multiple offers in 4-6 weeks → sells near asking price
- Tanglin unit list at $1.5M → may take 3 months to get first offer → seller accepts $1.4M (7% discount) to close deal

**Implication for investors:** Buy in high-volume projects. You'll exit faster and at prices closer to your expectations.

## Quantum Accessibility & Exit Audience

Quantum accessibility = the percentage of Singapore households able to afford the property price at sustainable debt levels.

The broader the accessible pool, the larger your future exit audience.

### Framework: Mapping Affordability to Household Capability

**Singapore household context:**
- Median household income: ~$10,000-12,000/month (mix of HDB, private occupants, investors)
- TDSR (Total Debt Service Ratio) limit: 60% of gross monthly income. This is the regulatory ceiling; most households target 50% to maintain buffer.
- LTV (Loan-to-Value) for private residential: Typically 75-80% for owner-occupants, lower for investors
- Maximum loan tenure: 30 years for most properties (35 years for some HDB, 25 years for remaining lease <30 years)

**Worked example:**
- Household income: $10,000/month
- TDSR capacity: 60% × $10,000 = $6,000/month for all debts
- Existing debts (car loan, credit card, student loan): ~$1,500/month
- Available for property mortgage: $6,000 - $1,500 = $4,500/month
- Max sustainable mortgage at 4.5% interest, 25-year tenor: ~$900,000
- With 20% down payment saved: Max purchase quantum = $1,125,000

**Implications:**
- Properties >$1.5M: Only accessible to households earning $18,000+/month (top 20-25% income bracket)
- Properties $800k-$1.5M: Accessible to households earning $12,000-18,000/month (middle-to-upper 30-40% of market)
- Properties $400k-$800k: Accessible to households earning $8,000-14,000/month (broad middle 40-50% of market)
- Properties <$400k: Accessible to broad swaths including first-time homebuyers, upgraders, investors (60-70% of market)

**Rule of thumb:** The broader the accessible quantum band, the larger your exit audience and the easier your sale.

### Exit Audience Breadth Analysis

Different property sizes and prices appeal to different buyer profiles. Understanding who buys when you sell is critical.

**1-Bedroom units ($250k-$600k entry range):**
- Buyer profiles: Singles, young couples, investors seeking rental yield, retirees downsizing
- Exit audience breadth: Very broad. Lowest price point attracts most diverse buyer pool.
- Rental appeal: Strong. Many tenants seek 1BR for affordability.
- Pricing power: Moderate. High volume but also high supply of 1BR stock.

**2-Bedroom units ($600k-$1.2M entry range):**
- Buyer profiles: Couples planning families, upgraders from 1BR, investors, young families
- Exit audience breadth: Broadest. "Goldilocks" size balancing affordability and space.
- Rental appeal: Strongest. 2BR units most demanded by renters (couple, family, investor-tenants).
- Pricing power: Strong. Highest transaction volume typically in 2BR segment.

**3-Bedroom units ($1.2M-$2.5M entry range):**
- Buyer profiles: Young families, upgraders from smaller units, investor-landlords seeking premium rental
- Exit audience breadth: Narrowing. Excludes first-time homebuyers without enough savings.
- Rental appeal: Moderate to strong depending on area. Tenant pool smaller than 2BR.
- Pricing power: Moderate. Transaction volume lower than 2BR proportionally.

**4-Bedroom+ units ($2.5M+ entry range):**
- Buyer profiles: Established families, multi-generational households, ultra-high-net-worth upgraders, owner-occupants only (few investors)
- Exit audience breadth: Narrow. Only top 10-15% of households can access this quantum.
- Rental appeal: Very weak. Few rental tenants demand 4BR at $5k-8k/month rents needed to justify price.
- Pricing power: Weak. Longest sales timelines, lowest volume relative to units on market.

**Caution:** Large 4BR units in non-prime areas are especially illiquid. Exit audience dries up when unit is large but area lacks amenities or school appeal.

### Foreigner Buying Restrictions

Understanding who is excluded from buying shapes exit audience:

**Properties accessible to foreigners:**
- Above $1 million purchase price
- Projects with no restriction (most private condos allow foreigners)
- ABSD (Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty) applies: ~20% of purchase price (effectively 20% cost + existing 4-8% stamp duty = 24-28% total transaction cost)

**Properties restricted from foreigners:**
- Below $1 million (exception: certain designated areas like POP units above $500k)
- HDB public housing (restricted to Singapore citizens/PRs)
- Certain conservation areas

**Implication for sellers:**
- If your property is below $1M, your exit audience is Singapore citizen/PR only (60-80% of total potential buyers)
- If your property is $1M+, foreigner investors and owner-occupants become part of exit audience (expands pool)
- Foreigner demand is cyclical; strengthens in booming markets, disappears in downturns
- Don't overly depend on foreigner demand for your exit

## Price Benchmarking Methodology

Price benchmarking is the core analytical tool. Compare your target to comps to understand if it's priced fairly.

### Step 1: Identify Comparable Projects

Comps = similar projects in terms of:
- **Location:** Same district (e.g., Novena) or adjacent district with similar profile (e.g., Bukit Timah to Tanglin)
- **Age:** Within 5-10 years. Projects built in different eras (1990s vs 2010s vs 2020s) have different standard finishes and appeal
- **Product type:** Condo, landed, cluster, executive condominium. Never compare condo to landed or EC directly.
- **Price tier:** Same quantum range ($400k-$800k or $1.2M-$2M). Wide quantum disparities create apples-to-oranges comparisons.

**Example comp set for "Novena Condo A" (3BR condo, built 2015, $1.8M listing):**
- Comp 1: "Novena Condo B" (3BR, built 2012, $1.75M) ✓ Same district, similar age, similar product, similar quantum
- Comp 2: "Thomson Condo" (3BR, built 2015, $1.85M) ✓ Adjacent district, similar age/product/quantum
- Comp 3: "Bukit Timah Condo" (3BR, built 2010, $2.1M) ✓ Nearby, similar age, but higher quantum — use with caution
- NOT: "Orchard Luxury Tower" (3BR, built 2018, $3.5M) ✗ Different tier, different era, different quantum
- NOT: "Punggol BTO" (3BR, 2020s, $700k) ✗ Different product type (HDB vs condo), different quantum

### Step 2: Compare PSF by Unit Type

Never compare overall project average PSF. Compare like-for-like unit types.

**Why this matters:**
- A project with many 1BR units may show lower average PSF than a project with many 4BR units, even if 3BR units are identically priced
- "Is this project cheaper?" requires unit-type-level comparison, not project-level average

**Methodology:**
1. Gather transaction data for 3BR units in your target project (last 5-8 transactions)
2. Calculate: Price ÷ Strata area (sqft) = PSF
3. Do the same for comps
4. Compare 3BR PSF in target vs comps

**Example:**
- Novena Condo A, 3BR unit: $1.8M ÷ 1,050 sqft = **$1,714 PSF**
- Novena Condo B, 3BR unit average: ($1.6M + $1.65M + $1.7M) ÷ 3 ÷ 1,000 sqft = **$1,650 PSF**
- Novena Condo A is priced **$64 PSF (3.8%) higher** than Condo B
- If Condo A and B are identical in layout/facility/location, this $64 PSF premium seems high. **Red flag.** Negotiation opportunity.

### Step 3: Compare Absolute Quantum

Some buyers shop by quantum (budget), not PSF. Absolute pricing matters.

**Framework:**
- If market shows 3BR units clustering at $1.65M-$1.85M, your $1.8M unit sits mid-range → fits market expectations
- If market shows $1.65M-$1.7M range, your $1.8M unit is outlier high → expect slower sales or need premium justification
- If market shows $1.85M-$2M range, your $1.8M unit is value → quick sale expected at asking price

**Implication:** Price positioning within the natural cluster matters. Being 10% above cluster average slows demand; being 5% below creates urgency.

### Step 4: New Launch vs Resale Gap Analysis

The gap between new launch prices and resale prices signals future appreciation potential.

**Framework:**
- **New launch premium 20-40% over resale:** Healthy gap. Resale has catch-up potential. New units will establish higher baseline for next resale cycle.
- **New launch premium <20% over resale:** Resale may be overvalued relative to new supply. Limited upside for resale; downside if new launches price lower.
- **New launch premium >40% over resale:** Resale very cheap relative to new. Strong catch-up potential but requires 2-3 year horizon to realise.
- **New launch trading below resale:** Distressed pricing or supply glut. Resale is overvalued; avoid buying at premium.

**Practical application:**
Your target is a 2015-built 3BR resale in Novena Condo A, asking $1.8M.
- New launch nearby ("Novena New Tower"): 3BR, $2.3M (28% premium)
- Other resale comps (2010-2014 builds) in area: $1.65M-$1.75M average
- Interpretation: New launch is pricing future market after supply absorption. Resale at $1.8M is slightly premium to peer average, but justified by new launch premium trajectory. Not overpriced.

### Step 5: Soon-to-TOP Projects as Pricing Bridges

Soon-to-TOP (Temporary Occupation Permit) projects are the link between new launch and mature resale. They show where prices are heading.

**Why soon-to-TOP projects matter:**
- New launch pricing = developer expectation 2-3 years out
- Soon-to-TOP pricing = market validation of new launch expectations (or rejection if projects struggle to sell)
- Mature resale pricing = old equilibrium
- Soon-to-TOP = current market consensus on where pricing should be

**Methodology:**
1. Identify soon-to-TOP projects (TOP expected within 6-18 months) in your target area
2. Review asking prices for similar unit types
3. Compare to your target property
4. Use as "predicted future value" gauge

**Example:**
- New launch 2BR in Punggol: $1.1M (2021 pricing)
- Soon-to-TOP 2BR in Punggol (TOP expected Feb 2026): $1.05M (2025 market validation)
- Current resale 2BR in Punggol (2014 building): $850k (2025 market)
- Interpretation: Market is repricing up from resale towards new launch; $1.05M soon-to-TOP validates upward momentum but also shows $1.1M new launch pricing may be slightly aggressive

## Facilities, Density, and Maintenance

Facilities quality, maintenance fees, and density correlate with long-term capital appreciation and owner satisfaction.

### Land Size vs Unit Count: Density Analysis

High-density projects (many units on limited land) may be cheaper to enter but offer fewer amenities and may feel crowded.

**Framework:**
- Calculate: Total unit count ÷ Land size (hectares) = units per hectare
- Compare to peer projects

**Density tiers:**
- **Low density (30-60 units/hectare):** Spacious feel, ample parking, good-sized facilities. Typical: cluster houses, small boutique condos.
- **Medium density (60-120 units/hectare):** Balanced. Typical private condos in mature estates.
- **High density (120-200 units/hectare):** Crowded feel, limited parking, shared amenity competition. Typical: CBD-fringe condos, certain HDB blocks.
- **Very high density (>200 units/hectare):** Extremely tight. Minimal facilities. Typical: old commercial-converted condos, some high-rise towers.

**Implication for resale:**
- Low-density projects maintain appeal over time (spacious feel resists commoditisation)
- High-density projects face commoditisation risk (cramped feel becomes liability as competition emerges)
- BUT: High-density near MRT can outperform if tenant demand is high (density trade-off for convenience)

**Maintenance fee correlation:**
- Higher density often = higher maintenance fees (more shared infrastructure, more wear)
- Example: 100-unit condo on 1 hectare pays ~$0.20/sqft monthly vs 300-unit on same hectare pays $0.30-0.35/sqft monthly

### Facilities Quality Assessment

Facilities influence owner satisfaction and rental appeal.

**Tier 1 — Standard facilities (all projects should have):**
- Swimming pool (1-2)
- Gymnasium / fitness center
- Function/community room
- Playground (if family-oriented)
- Car park (1.0-1.5 spaces per unit typical)

**Tier 2 — Enhanced facilities (strong projects have):**
- Sauna / steam room
- Basketball/tennis court
- Yoga/pilates studio
- Co-working space (newer projects)
- Maid's room/helper's quarters

**Tier 3 — Premium facilities (luxury/flagship projects):**
- Spa / wellness centre
- Golf simulator / driving range
- Cinema / entertainment lounge
- Rooftop garden / sky terrace
- Pet park / pet spa

**Relevance to target demographic:**
- Family-focused project: Should have playground, childcare options, community room → ask if these are present
- Young professional project: Should have gym, yoga, co-working → check if built-out
- Investor/expat project: Maid's quarters, parking abundance → critical amenities

**Quality assessment:** Visit the project, evaluate:
- Are facilities clean and well-maintained?
- Are they under-utilised (poor design) or well-used (good design)?
- Are they outdated (original 2010 gym vs recently renovated)?
- Outdated facilities drag down rental appeal and owner perception

### Maintenance Fee Benchmarking

Maintenance fees (sinking funds) are ongoing costs that impact net yield for investors and quality of life for owner-occupants.

**Typical maintenance fee ranges (per sqft per month):**
- **Low:** $0.15-0.20/sqft (older projects, lower density, limited amenities)
- **Moderate:** $0.20-0.35/sqft (typical private condos, medium amenities)
- **High:** $0.35-0.50/sqft (new projects with extensive amenities, or older projects with poor cost control)
- **Very high:** $0.50+/sqft (luxury towers, aging projects with escalating costs, or poor management)

**Worked example:**
- 1,000 sqft unit at $0.25/sqft = $250/month = $3,000/year
- Same unit at $0.40/sqft = $400/month = $4,800/year (60% higher cost)
- Over 10 years: $30,000 vs $48,000 difference

**Red flag scenarios:**
- Maintenance fees much higher than peer projects (possible mismanagement or aging infrastructure requiring costly repairs)
- Fees rising >5% annually (unsustainable cost growth; reserve fund may be inadequate)
- Sinking fund contributions seem very low (<$0.10/sqft): Project may face future special levies for major repairs

**Best practice:** Compare target project's maintenance fee to 3-4 peer projects. If 20%+ higher without corresponding amenity quality, negotiate or walk away.

## Lease Profile Analysis

Remaining lease determines financing eligibility, lender appetite, and ultimate time-horizon for your property.

### Remaining Lease Impact on Financing

Banks restrict loan tenors and LTV based on remaining lease. This creates hard financing walls.

**Financing thresholds (as of 2025 — always verify with lender):**

**75+ years remaining:**
- LTV: 80% (owner-occupant), 60-75% (investor)
- Loan tenure: Up to 30-35 years
- TDSR calculation: Full debt service applies
- Example: $1M property, 80% LTV = $800k loan available, $2,400/month payment (4.5%, 30-year), no depreciation factor
- Financing: Fully accessible to all buyers

**60-75 years remaining:**
- LTV: 75-80% (owner-occupant), 50-60% (investor)
- Loan tenure: Up to 25-30 years (lenders may cap tenure shorter)
- TDSR: Full debt service applies
- Example: $1M property, 75% LTV = $750k loan available, standard calculations apply
- Financing: Accessible but tighter; investor LTV drops 10-15%

**40-60 years remaining:**
- LTV: 60-70% (owner-occupant), 40-50% (investor)
- Loan tenure: Up to 20-25 years (some lenders cap at 20)
- TDSR: Depreciation factor may apply (e.g., property value declining as lease shortens)
- Example: $1M property, 60% LTV = $600k loan, down from 75-80% at longer lease
- Financing: Restricted. Investor financing particularly tight. Down payment requirement high (40-50%).

**<40 years remaining:**
- LTV: <60% (owner-occupant), 30-40% (investor)
- Loan tenure: 15-20 years or less (some lenders won't lend)
- TDSR: Depreciation factor definitely applies
- Example: $1M property, 50% LTV = $500k loan only
- Financing: Very restricted. Mostly cash buyers or large down-payment buyers. Investor exit audience essentially zero.

**Critical impact:** As lease shortens, financing width collapses. A property with 50 years remaining is 50% less financeable than 75+ years. This shrinks exit audience materially.

### Bala's Curve Concept

Remaining lease impacts property value according to a curve first popularised by analyst Bala Shanmugam.

**The curve:**
- 99 to 80 years: Minimal value impact. Properties depreciate slowly, <0.5% annually
- 80 to 60 years: Moderate depreciation. ~1-2% per year as financing begins restricting
- 60 to 40 years: Accelerating depreciation. ~3-4% per year as investor exit audience disappears
- 40 to 20 years: Steep depreciation. 4-6% per year; cash buyers only; value becomes residual
- <20 years: Extreme depreciation. Properties are essentially wasting assets

**Practical example:**
- Property bought at 95 years remaining for $1M
- 10 years later (85 years remaining): Comparable value ~$950k (minimal depreciation)
- 20 years later (75 years remaining): Comparable value ~$900k (slight depreciation)
- 30 years later (65 years remaining): Comparable value ~$800k (steeper depreciation kicks in)
- 40 years later (55 years remaining): Comparable value ~$550k (sharp depreciation)

**Investor implication:** The back-end of Bala's curve (below 60 years) destroys capital. If you're investing with 10-15 year hold horizon, ensure property starts with >75 years remaining. Otherwise, you're fighting depreciation headwinds on top of normal market returns.

### Freehold vs 99-Year Leasehold

Freehold properties have no lease expiration; 99-year leasehold has a finite life.

**Freehold characteristics:**
- Price premium: Typically trades 10-20% higher than comparable 99-year property
- Tax advantage: Freehold not subject to lease depreciation
- Financing: No lease depreciation concerns; full LTV/tenure always available
- Long-term appeal: Intergenerational asset (can pass to children indefinitely)
- Reality check: Freeholds in Singapore are rare (5-10% of private stock), mostly in established core areas

**99-year leasehold characteristics:**
- Price discount: Trades 10-20% lower than comparable freehold
- Tax disadvantage: Lease depreciation kicks in, especially below 75 years
- Financing: Subject to lease restrictions (see above)
- Time horizon: Finite (99 years ~ 4 generations, but impact accelerates after year 70)
- Reality check: 99-year is the vast majority of private residential in Singapore

**Does freehold always outperform?**
No. A freehold in a declining area may underperform a 99-year in a strong growth area. Freehold is a positive but not determinative. Focus on area fundamentals first; freehold is a tie-breaker.

**Practical assessment:** If comparing 99-year and freehold at different price points, calculate: Is the freehold premium justified by area strength and rent potential? Don't overpay for freehold if area is weak.

### En-Bloc Potential and Lease Life

En-bloc (collective sale) potential increases with certain factors:

**Factors increasing en-bloc probability:**
- Large land size relative to plot ratio utilisation (developer can build higher)
- Prime location (land value appreciation justifies replacing old building)
- Lease <80 years (owners increasingly interested in reset)
- Developer interest (tender results show strong bids)
- Building age >30 years with costly major repairs
- Collective sale history (successful case in district builds confidence)

**Example:** A 1990s development on 2-hectare land in Tiong Bahru with 60+ year remaining lease in a transformation area has high en-bloc probability. New development could build taller, creating value spread.

**Investor consideration:** High en-bloc potential is a double-edged sword. It's a positive for long-term value (reset lease), but uncertain timing creates hold-period risk. Don't buy purely on en-bloc expectation unless area is strong regardless.

## Developer Track Record Assessment

For new launches, developer reputation impacts delivery quality, defects, and after-sales service.

### Past Project Defects & Quality Issues

Research developer's past projects:
- **Check:** ACRA (Agency for Corporate Affairs) records, public disputes, warranty claim frequencies
- **Ask:** On-site inspection — are past projects well-maintained? Leaking balconies? Rusting grilles?
- **Browse:** Property forums (PropertyGuru, 99.co forums) — do owners complain about defects, poor contractor work?
- **Pattern:** Some developers consistently deliver quality (e.g., City Developments Limited, CapitaLand, Keppel); others have spotty records

**Red flags:**
- Multiple past projects with warranty disputes
- Major structural defects reported
- Poor maintenance visible in older properties
- Negative online reputation among owners

### Completion Timeline Adherence

Delays impact your cash flow planning and carrying cost.

- Check: Developer's past projects — were TOPs delivered on time?
- Pattern: Some developers (established Singapore developers) rarely delay; others (smaller/foreign developers) have poor track records
- Caution: Progressive payment schemes mean delays = longer capital deployment; budget for slippage

### After-Sales Service Reputation

Post-TOP, you depend on developer for warranty claims and building management transition.

- Check: Warranty structure. Is it backed by insurer or just developer goodwill?
- Check: How responsive is developer to defect claims?
- Pattern: Established developers typically have better service; boutique developers may be less responsive

### Major Developer Categories in Singapore

**Tier 1 (Excellent track record):**
- City Developments Ltd (CDL), Keppel Land, CapitaLand, UOL, Oxley
- Characteristics: Rare delays, quality construction, excellent after-sales

**Tier 2 (Good track record):**
- Bukit Sembilan, Hoi Hup, Fusionopolis, Poly Development
- Characteristics: Generally reliable, occasional delays, adequate quality

**Tier 3 (Variable track record):**
- Smaller regional developers, joint ventures
- Characteristics: Spotty delivery, quality varies, limited after-sales

**Foreign developers:**
- Vary widely; some (Japanese, Korean) excellent; some (speculative investors) poor
- Caution: If developer is not Singapore-based, verify financial stability (will they be around to honour warranty?)

## Surrounding Projects & Market Dynamics

A project doesn't exist in isolation. Nearby developments affect your property's future pricing.

### How Neighbouring Developments Impact Your Project

**Competing developments (same type, same subzone):**
- New condo launches nearby = price ceiling pressure for resale in 2-3 years post-TOP
- Multiple launches clustered = inventory glut; prices forced down until absorption complete
- Same price point (e.g., two $1M projects competing for same 3BR buyer) = direct competition

**Complementary developments (different type or different subzone):**
- EC launch (cheaper alternative) = may pull budget-conscious buyers away from your private project
- HDB BTO launch = may reduce upgrader demand for low-end private units ($400k-600k range)
- Landed house launch = may pull family buyer demand away from large condos

**Enhancement developments (new amenities/infrastructure nearby):**
- New mall opening = amenity enhancement, positive for your project
- MRT station opening = catalytic; positive for your project's rental and capital appreciation
- School relocation away = negative for your project (especially if school had driven premium)

### Timing Dynamics: Early vs Late Cycles

**Projects launching early in supply cycle:**
- Benefit: Less direct competition; price discovery favorable to early launches
- Risk: Later launches may undercut on price, creating reference point for market
- Strategy: Early launch projects need strong marketing and brand to maintain premium

**Projects launching late in supply cycle:**
- Benefit: Later launches can observe market absorption of earlier projects and price accordingly
- Risk: If early projects struggled to absorb, late launches face ceiling
- Strategy: Late projects often discount to move volume quickly

**Your project in context:**
- If your project is a first-mover in area: Enjoy pricing power but face risk of later launches undercutting
- If your project is a late-mover: Expect to be priced lower but face less competition
- If your project is in saturated area: Prepare for modest pricing and longer sales cycles
