# Framework Synthesis: How This Skill Adapts Leading Singapore Property Methodologies

This skill synthesises contributions from multiple leading Singapore property analysts and platforms. Understanding these sources and how they've been adapted provides context for the analytical framework.

---

## Eric Chiew's Contributions

Eric Chiew is a prolific Singapore property analyst, educator, and thought leader with deep expertise in property investing, portfolio strategy, and market analysis.

### Adapted Framework: "Right Area, Right Project, Right Unit, Right Price"

**Eric's core principle:** Property success requires excellence across all four dimensions. Failure in any one can derail returns.

**Original formulation:** Chiew emphasises that buyers often shortcut this analysis, focusing narrowly (e.g., "I want a condo in Bukit Timah") and then picking the first acceptable unit. This leads to overpricing or poor project selection.

**How we adapted it:** We restructured this as a **top-down funnel** (Area → Project → Unit → Price):
- **Area:** Is this location fundamentally sound? (Master Plan, catalysts, supply, amenities)
- **Project:** Does this project have good liquidity, broad appeal, and stable pricing? (Transaction volume, exit audience, facilities)
- **Unit:** Within the chosen project, is this unit offering value? (Price positioning, stack quality, layout)
- **Price:** Is the asking price justified by all previous layers of analysis? (PSF benchmarking, premium justification)

The funnel forces early elimination of bad areas before wasting time on project/unit analysis.

### Adapted Framework: Optionality Awareness

**Eric's principle:** Don't fixate on a single property. Map the landscape of what the same quantum buys across different regions and product types.

**Original formulation:** Chiew advocates awareness of "what you could get elsewhere for the same price." This prevents tunnel vision and overpaying.

**How we adapted it:** We integrated optionality into the **Price Benchmarking** section:
- Compare your target not just to comps in the same area, but to comparable units in nearby areas (nearby RCR, adjacent OCR)
- Quantify: "For $1.5M, am I getting better value in Novena 3BR vs Thomson 3BR vs Bukit Timah 2BR?"
- This forces you to articulate why you're choosing your specific unit/area vs alternatives at the same quantum

**Practical application in skill:** When benchmarking prices, we ask: "What does this quantum buy in competing areas?" This is optionality mapping.

### Adapted Framework: Progressive Upgrading & Portfolio Thinking

**Eric's principle:** Each property purchase should position you strategically for a stronger next move. Properties are steps in a wealth-building journey, not standalone investments.

**Original formulation:** Chiew discusses the idea of "property progression" — thoughtfully upgrading from starter property to next tier, each move building equity and borrowing capacity for the next move.

**How we adapted it:** We emphasise **portfolio thinking** in the recommendation section:
- Is this property a standalone investment, or does it position you for a stronger next move?
- Example: First-time buyer in OCR. Good strategy if OCR is undervalued relative to RCR and you plan to leverage equity to upgrade in 3-5 years.
- Example: Upgrader buying in RCR. Good strategy if entry price is disciplined (not paying premium) and area has multi-year appreciation runway ahead.

**Practical application in skill:** When recommending for upgraders, we assess: "Does this property set you up well for the next upgrade?" This is portfolio thinking.

### Adapted Framework: Entry Price Discipline

**Eric's principle:** Entry price is everything. Overpay at entry, and you compress returns for the entire holding period.

**Original formulation:** Chiew repeatedly emphasises that the first decision (how much to pay) cascades through all future decisions (holding period, exit timing, ultimate profit).

**How we adapted it:** This is a core principle in our **Unit-Level Analysis** and **Price Benchmarking** sections:
- We mandate comparison to recent transactions in same unit type, same project, same district
- We penalise asking prices that sit above comparable comps without clear justification
- We force you to articulate: "Why is this unit 5-10% more expensive than the recent comparable sale?"

**Practical application in skill:** Every price recommendation includes a "is this entry price justified?" assessment. Unjustified premiums are red flags.

## PropertyLimBrothers (PLB) MOAT Framework

PropertyLimBrothers is a YouTube channel and media brand known for data-driven property analysis. Their MOAT (Momentum, Optionality, Affordability, Tenure) framework has influenced many Singapore investors.

### Adapted Framework: The Multi-Factor Scoring Approach

**PLB's contribution:** Rather than assessing properties on single factors (price, location, schools), PLB advocates a multi-factor scorecard. Each factor is ranked; overall picture emerges from factor combination.

**Original MOAT components:**
- **M (Momentum):** Is the area gaining buyer/tenant momentum? Price/volume trends positive?
- **O (Optionality):** Does this property appeal to broad exit audience?
- **A (Affordability):** Is quantum accessible to a broad pool of potential buyers?
- **T (Tenure):** Is lease profile healthy?

**How we adapted it:** We expanded MOAT into a **5-tier scoring framework** across three levels (Area, Project, Unit):

**Our tiers:**
- Very Strong, Strong, Moderate, Weak, Very Weak (vs. PLB's simpler Yes/No or scoring scales)

**Our factor expansion:** We adapted PLB's core MOAT ideas but expanded them into specific sub-factors:
- **Momentum** → Our "Area Trend Context" (10-year price/volume trends, district classification)
- **Optionality** → Our "Exit Audience Breadth" (who buys when you sell?)
- **Affordability** → Our "Quantum Accessibility" (what % of households can afford?)
- **Tenure** → Our "Lease Profile Analysis" (financing thresholds, Bala's curve)
- **Additional factors** we added: Schools (PLB-inspired but our emphasis), Facilities (PLB's "density" concept expanded), Transaction Liquidity (PLB's momentum proxy)

**Key difference:** PLB uses numeric scoring (e.g., M=8/10, O=7/10); we use tier labels with mandatory evidence. Both approaches work; tiers are more narrative-friendly; numeric is more comparable.

### Adapted Framework: Volume, Quantum, Exit Audience Effects

**PLB's contribution:** PLB identifies specific multiplier effects that compound returns:

1. **Volume Effect:** High-volume projects have better price discovery, tighter bid-ask spreads, faster exits
2. **Quantum Effect:** Properties accessible to broad income bands have wider exit audience
3. **Exit Audience Breadth:** The wider the buyer pool when you sell, the better your exit timing/pricing power

**How we adapted it:** These map directly into our framework:
- **Volume Effect** → "Transaction Volume & Liquidity" (project-level analysis)
- **Quantum Effect** → "Quantum Accessibility & Exit Audience Breadth" (project-level analysis)
- **Exit Audience Breadth** → Detailed assessment of who buys 1BR/2BR/3BR/4BR units by profile

**Practical application in skill:** When scoring projects, we weight transaction volume and quantum accessibility heavily. These are multiplier effects that compound over holding period.

### Adapted Framework: Landsize Density & Regional Disparity

**PLB's contribution:** PLB notes that landsize-to-unit ratio (density) affects facilities, maintenance costs, and premium/discount profile. Additionally, comparing across regions reveals pricing disparities that signal opportunity.

**How we adapted it:**
- **Density analysis:** We cover this in "Facilities & Density" (unit count ÷ land size, density tiers)
- **Regional disparity:** We integrate this into "Price Benchmarking" (compare PSF across CCR/RCR/OCR, adjacent districts)

**Practical application in skill:** We ask: "Is this project at optimal density for its product type?" Overcrowded projects may show slower appreciation; spacious projects command premium over time.

### Adapted Framework: Parents Attraction Effect

**PLB's contribution:** PLB identifies that school proximity drives measurable property premium. Families seeking elite schools create demand spikes in nearby areas.

**How we adapted it:** We created a comprehensive "School Proximity Analysis" section covering:
- The 1km registration rule and its impact
- Elite school premium (5-20% typical)
- GEP/SAP school significance
- Research on premium magnitude
- School relocation case study (Nanyang Primary impact example)

**Practical application in skill:** We assess area-level school factors as high-weight element for family buyers. Schools are a material appreciation driver in family-oriented areas.

## Stacked Homes' Contributions

Stacked Homes is an editorial platform known for detailed project reviews and data-driven analysis.

### Adapted Framework: Project-Level Deep Dives

**Stacked Homes' contribution:** Stacked Homes publishes comprehensive project reviews analysing every aspect: location, facilities, pricing, developer reputation, unit-by-unit layout analysis.

**How we adapted it:** We created a comprehensive "Project-Level Analysis" section covering:
- Transaction volume analysis (project liquidity)
- Facilities and density (unit-level analysis of every project element)
- Lease profile considerations (financing implications)
- Developer track record assessment
- Surrounding projects impact

**Practical application in skill:** Our project-level section goes deep on factors that determine exit audience breadth and pricing sustainability. This mirrors Stacked Homes' depth.

### Adapted Framework: Unit-by-Unit Comparative Analysis

**Stacked Homes' contribution:** Stacked Homes excels at comparing units within same project — which stack offers value? Which is overpriced?

**How we adapted it:** We created "Unit-Level Analysis" with focus on:
- Stack analysis (floor, facing, view corridor, noise)
- Layout efficiency (PSF, balcony proportion, bedroom sizes)
- Price positioning within project
- New launch stack selection strategy (ballot, value identification)

**Practical application in skill:** This mirrors Stacked Homes' unit-by-unit deep dive. We ask: "Which units in this project are value outliers?"

### Adapted Framework: Liveability and Practical Assessment

**Stacked Homes' contribution:** Stacked Homes assesses not just investment metrics but liveability — can you actually live in this unit comfortably? Are finishes quality? Are neighbours compatible?

**How we adapted it:** While our skill is investor-focused, we incorporated practical unit assessment:
- Layout efficiency and bedroom sizes (can furniture fit comfortably?)
- Facing and natural ventilation (will you be comfortable living there?)
- Neighbourhood completeness (are all daily amenities within reach?)
- Noise exposure (will you enjoy quiet evenings?)

**Practical application in skill:** We don't just ask "Will this appreciate?" but also "Will you enjoy living here?" This is Stacked Homes' influence.

## Data Platform Contributions: 99.co, PropertyGuru, EdgeProp, SRX, RealSmart

These platforms have democratised property data access. Previously, only professionals with REALIS access could do detailed analysis. These platforms have made sophisticated analysis accessible to retail buyers.

### Adapted Framework: Transaction Data Accessibility

**Their contribution:** These platforms aggregate transaction data, making it accessible without expensive REALIS subscriptions.

**How we adapted it:** Our "Data Sources" section recommends tier-appropriate data access:
- Casual buyers: 99.co free + Stacked Homes articles
- Serious buyers: Add PropertyGuru + X-Value valuation checks
- Investors: Add REALIS + EdgeProp profitability analysis + RealSmart tracking

**Practical application in skill:** We acknowledge that professional-grade analysis is now accessible at retail price points. No buyer needs to do analysis blind.

### Adapted Framework: Profitability and Gain/Loss Tracking

**EdgeProp's contribution:** EdgeProp publishes gain/loss data by cohort (e.g., "How much did 2015 Novena buyers profit?"). This transparency is new.

**How we adapted it:** We reference profitability analysis in "Price Benchmarking" section:
- What returns did comparable cohorts achieve?
- Is your entry price positioning you for similar/better returns?

**Practical application in skill:** Instead of guessing expected returns, buyers can reference actual cohort performance. This informs expected appreciation ranges.

### Adapted Framework: Automated Valuation as Sanity Check

**SRX X-Value's contribution:** Sophisticated machine-learning valuations provide quick sanity checks. Not gospel, but useful validation.

**How we adapted it:** We recommend X-Value as a checkpoint:
- Developer asking $2M for unit you analysed
- X-Value estimates $1.8M
- Gap suggests possible overpricing; warrants negotiation

**Practical application in skill:** Valuations are used as input to price benchmarking, not as substitute for detailed analysis.

### Adapted Framework: Investment Metrics and ROI Tracking

**RealSmart and EdgeProp's contribution:** These platforms operationalise investment metrics (ROI, rental yield, capital gains tracking) in dashboards.

**How we adapted it:** We reference these tools for tracking but keep the skill focused on decision-making (should I buy?) rather than post-purchase management.

**Practical application in skill:** Data platforms help execute; analysis skill helps decide. We focus on the latter.

## Our Unique Synthesis: What Makes This Framework Different

### 1. Top-Down Funnel vs Side-by-Side Comparison

**Most Singapore frameworks:** Do side-by-side property comparison (Project A vs B vs C, all at same time).

**Our adaptation:** Area → Project → Unit funnel forces elimination at each stage. Bad areas eliminated before project time investment. This is more efficient and less prone to anchoring bias.

### 2. 5-Tier Scoring with Mandatory Evidence

**PLB framework:** Numeric scoring (8/10, 7/10, etc.). Clean but potentially arbitrary.

**Our adaptation:** Tier-based (Very Strong to Very Weak) with mandatory evidence support. Forces explicitness: "Why is this area Very Strong?" Harder to BS; easier to challenge.

### 3. Buyer Profile Weighting

**Most frameworks:** One-size-fits-all scoring.

**Our adaptation:** Factor weightings explicitly adjust by buyer profile:
- Family buyer: Weight schools and space heavily
- Investor: Weight rental yield and tenant pool
- Upgrader: Weight exit audience and price appreciation potential
- Retiree: Weight lease profile and walkability

Same property scores differently depending on buyer profile. More realistic.

### 4. Emphasis on Exit Audience Breadth

**Most frameworks:** Talk about "demand" generically.

**Our adaptation:** We operationalise "exit audience" explicitly:
- Who buys 1BR units when you sell? (Singles, investors)
- Who buys 2BR? (Couples, investor-landlords, young families)
- Who buys 3BR/4BR? (Families, upgraders, multi-generational)
- How many people fit each category?
- Broad appeal = lower exit risk

### 5. Portfolio Thinking

**Most frameworks:** Analyse each property standalone.

**Our adaptation:** We ask "What's the role of this property in your portfolio?"
- Starter property? Good if value-priced and area is rising
- Second property? Should add different characteristics/location diversification
- Upgrader? Should set you up for next-tier move
- Pure investment? Should maximise yield + capital appreciation potential

### 6. Challenge Assumptions

**Most frameworks:** List strengths; assume buyer will weigh them.

**Our adaptation:** We force identification of weaknesses:
- "What could go wrong with this investment?"
- "What risks are embedded?"
- "How confident should you be in the recommendation?"

This discourages blind enthusiasm and anchoring.
