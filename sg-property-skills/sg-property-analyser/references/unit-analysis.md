# Unit-Level Analysis Framework

Unit-level analysis is the final zoom before making a purchase decision. The right unit in the right project in the right area can outperform a mediocre unit significantly, even when all three are in same district.

## Entry Price Positioning

Entry price should reflect the unit's characteristics relative to peers. Overpaying at entry shrinks all future returns.

### Comparing Unit PSF to Relevant Benchmarks

**Benchmark 1: Project average PSF for same unit type**

Calculate the average PSF for all 3BR units sold in your target project in the past 12 months.

- Gather transaction data: Recent 3BR sales in your project
- Calculate: Price ÷ Strata area for each
- Average the PSF
- Compare your target unit's asking PSF to this average

**Example:**
- Novena Condo A 3BR recent sales (past 12 months):
  - Unit 01-05: $1.65M ÷ 1,020 sqft = $1,617/sqft
  - Unit 15-08: $1.72M ÷ 1,050 sqft = $1,638/sqft
  - Unit 22-03: $1.68M ÷ 1,030 sqft = $1,631/sqft
  - Average: **$1,629/sqft**
- Your target (Unit 18-07): $1.8M ÷ 1,050 sqft = **$1,714/sqft**
- Asking premium: $85/sqft or **5.2% above project average**
- Question: Is your unit's location/floor/facing worth 5.2% premium?

**Benchmark 2: Same-stack or same-floor level history**

If data available, compare to the same stack's past transactions.

- Higher floors typically show 1-3% premium per floor (especially floors 1-10; premium tapers above)
- South-facing units may show 2-5% premium vs north-facing in same building
- Corner units typically 5-10% premium vs non-corner

**Example:**
- Unit 18-07 (your target) is 18th floor, south-facing, corner unit, asking $1,714/sqft
- Unit 18-02 (same floor, non-corner, north-facing) sold 6 months ago at $1,650/sqft
- Your premium analysis:
  - Corner premium: ~5-7% = $80-115/sqft
  - South vs north facing: ~3% = $50/sqft
  - Implied value for 18-07: $1,650 + $130 = **$1,780/sqft** (mid-point)
  - Your asking: $1,714/sqft
  - Your unit actually trades at **$66/sqft discount** to implied value
  - **This is good value.** Negotiate from here; has room to move.

### District Average PSF Comparison

Compare your unit's PSF to the broader district average for same unit type.

**Why this matters:** Project-level variations exist, but district-level trends show whether entire district is expensive or cheap.

**Framework:**
- District average 3BR PSF: Query PropertyGuru/99.co for past 12-month sales in district. Example: Novena district 3BR average $1,650/sqft
- Your project: $1,629/sqft (in-line or slightly below district average)
- Your unit: $1,714/sqft (above both project and district average)
- Interpretation: Your unit is priced at premium to both comps. **Ask why.** If the justification is weak, negotiate.

### Testing the Price: Rule of Thumb Comparisons

Quick sanity check: What does the same quantum buy elsewhere?

**Framework:**
- Your target: 3BR, $1.8M, Novena Condo A
- Same quantum in Bukit Timah: Often buys 3BR + 1BR (larger unit) at same price, or smaller 3BR with better location
- Same quantum in Punggol: Often buys 4BR or larger 3BR
- Same quantum in Tiong Bahru (similar tier): Often buys similar 3BR but with better heritage/amenity profile

**Implication:** If $1.8M in Novena only buys comparable 3BR, but $1.8M elsewhere buys larger/better unit, Novena pricing may be high relative to optionality.

This isn't a reason to reject, but **forces you to articulate why Novena justifies the quantum** (e.g., "Superior schools, better MRT proximity, I prefer the neighbourhood"). Don't buy premium quantum just because you've anchored on the unit.

## Stack Analysis for New Launches

New launch buying is unique because you're choosing from developer floor plans, not inspecting completed units. Stack analysis is critical.

### Facing Analysis: Cardinal Directions and Implications

**North-facing:**
- Advantage: Consistent indirect sunlight (no harsh afternoon heat)
- Advantage: Cooler interiors in afternoon
- Disadvantage: Less natural light (can feel darker)
- Premium adjustment: Typically baseline/neutral (no premium, no discount)
- Tenant appeal: Strong (comfort-focused renters like cooler units)

**South-facing:**
- Advantage: Abundant natural light
- Advantage: Warmer interiors (may be desirable in cooler climate months)
- Disadvantage: Intense afternoon sun (can overheat, require heavy curtaining)
- Disadvantage: Glare (can be uncomfortable without mitigation)
- Premium adjustment: +3-5% premium typical
- Tenant appeal: Moderate (some prefer light, others dislike heat)

**East-facing:**
- Advantage: Morning sunlight (naturally energising)
- Advantage: Afternoon is cooler (morning sun ends by noon)
- Disadvantage: Early wake-up from direct sun (not ideal for night-shift workers)
- Premium adjustment: +1-3% premium typical
- Tenant appeal: Strong for early risers; weak for night-shift professionals

**West-facing:**
- Advantage: Afternoon sunlight (bright interiors, naturally warm)
- Disadvantage: Intense afternoon heat (2pm-6pm is brutally hot)
- Disadvantage: Requires heavy air-conditioning; high energy bills
- Premium adjustment: -3-8% discount typical
- Tenant appeal: Weak (tenants avoid west-facing unless deeply discounted)

**Practical implication for new launches:** If buying on floor plan without seeing actual units:
- Prioritise north/south-facing for better resale appeal
- East-facing acceptable, especially higher floors (less afternoon obstruction)
- Avoid west-facing unless significant price discount (10%+) justifies the heat/energy trade-off

### View Corridor Analysis: What Blocks Future Views?

Check the URA Master Plan and site plans for what's zoned directly in front of your unit.

**Scenario 1: Frontage is open/park/green:**
- View: Open, unobstructed
- Future obstruction risk: Very Low (unlikely someone builds in front of park)
- Premium: Justified to hold higher expectations

**Scenario 2: Frontage is medium-density residential zoning (PR 2.5-3.0):**
- View: Currently open if land not yet built; future development likely
- Future obstruction risk: Moderate (plot may be developed within 5-7 years, creating obstruction)
- Premium: Discount for obstruction risk; don't pay "open view" premium for future-obstruction risk
- Strategy: Account for future obstruction in pricing; don't anchor on current view

**Scenario 3: Frontage is high-density zoning (PR 4.5+) or commercial zoning (towers likely):**
- View: Expect obstruction within 5 years
- Future obstruction risk: Very High
- Premium: Pay no view premium; assume obstruction will occur
- Strategy: Buy for location/amenity, not views; view is temporary bonus

**Data source:** Check URA Master Plan and Development Guide Plan for your precinct. These show zoning and plot ratio of land in front of your unit.

**Practical example:**
- Your unit at "Novena Condo A, Unit 25-03"
- DGP shows plot ratio 3.5 residential zoning frontage
- Frontage land is currently a car park / low-rise shophouse
- Assessment: Extremely high probability this frontage develops into 5-8 storey residential within 5-10 years
- Recommendation: If unit is premium-priced for "open view," negotiate down or avoid. View is temporary.

### Noise Exposure: Sources and Impact

Noise sensitivity varies by buyer. Consider noise sources:

**Major noise sources affecting units:**

**Proximity to major roads (500m-1km):**
- Impact: Consistent traffic hum, especially morning (6-9am) and evening (4-8pm) peak hours
- Decibel level: ~70-80 dB (similar to vacuum cleaner noise)
- How to test: Visit unit at peak hour (evening 5-7pm is worst) to experience
- Mitigation: Double-glazed windows, acoustic insulation
- Premium adjustment: 2-5% discount typical for road-proximate units
- Tenant impact: Some tenants tolerate, others avoid; leasing slightly slower

**Proximity to MRT tracks (200-400m):**
- Impact: Train rumble every 3-5 minutes during peak hours
- Decibel level: ~85-90 dB (loud; makes conversation difficult)
- How to test: Visit during morning peak (7-9am) when frequency is highest
- Mitigation: Limited; acoustic windows help but don't eliminate
- Premium adjustment: 5-10% discount typical for MRT-proximate units
- Tenant impact: Significant impact on leasing; 10-20% lower rents common
- Special consideration: Within 400m of MRT tracks (not just station) is problematic; beyond 500m usually acceptable

**Proximity to schools (100-400m):**
- Impact: Morning (7-8am) drop-off, afternoon (1-3pm) pickup noise, occasional sports days (Saturday mornings)
- Decibel level: ~65-75 dB (moderate; distracting but not unbearable)
- How to test: Visit unit on school day during school hours
- Mitigation: Acceptable windows, good insulation
- Premium adjustment: Varies by buyer; some value school proximity, others discount for noise
- Tenant impact: Family tenants may tolerate or even prefer (school proximity); young professionals avoid

**Proximity to major construction sites:**
- Impact: Temporary (typically 2-5 years); intermittent noise during work hours
- How to assess: Check URA pipeline and Master Plan for future development on adjacent land
- Caution: Avoid buying units facing active construction site or pre-construction land; noise + dust is significant

**Practical noise assessment for new launches:**
- Visit showunit at peak hour (evening 5-7pm) to experience ambient noise
- Walk outside the unit building to assess MRT/road proximity
- Check Google Maps satellite view to identify construction sites, schools, major roads within 500m
- If noise sources are present, expect 5-10% discount vs comparable units without noise

### Afternoon Sun and Energy Bills

West-facing units suffer extreme afternoon heat. This impacts tenant satisfaction and energy costs.

**Impact framework:**
- West-facing units in afternoon (2pm-6pm) can reach 28-32°C indoors even with air-con running
- Air-con energy consumption 30-50% higher for west-facing units vs north-facing
- Tenant perception: "Hot and expensive to cool" = negative
- Renting impact: Harder to lease, lower rents acceptable

**Mitigation options:**
- External shading (louvers, screens) — reduces heat 20-30%, costs $2k-5k
- Window films — reduces heat 15-25%, cost $500-1.5k, but can feel dark
- Premium window film — reduces heat 30-40%, costs $5k+, risk of aesthetic impact

**Practical decision:** If west-facing unit is 8-10% cheaper than east/north alternatives, the discount may be justified by tenant appeal impact. If discount is only 2-3%, avoid west-facing.

## Layout Efficiency

Layout efficiency determines usable living space relative to purchase price. A unit that wastes 20% on balconies and corridors is less valuable than one with 90% efficiency.

### Strata Area vs Saleable Area Ratio

**Definitions:**
- **Strata area:** Total area of your unit inclusive of balconies, utility areas, walls
- **Saleable area:** Living/usable area exclusive of balconies, walls, utility spaces
- **Efficiency ratio:** Saleable ÷ Strata (e.g., 800 saleable ÷ 1,000 strata = 80% efficiency)

**Typical efficiency ratios:**
- **Excellent:** 85%+ saleable-to-strata ratio. Nearly all space is usable; minimal wasted area.
- **Good:** 80-84% ratio. Typical for well-designed modern condos.
- **Acceptable:** 75-79% ratio. Some wasted space but acceptable.
- **Poor:** <75% ratio. Excessive balconies, corridors, utility areas. Poor value.

**How to calculate:**
- Ask developer for both strata and saleable area in floor plan
- Calculate ratio: Saleable ÷ Strata
- Compare to peer projects (check floor plans)

**Implication:** A unit with 80% efficiency at $1,650/sqft (strata) = $2,062/sqft (saleable). Same unit with 75% efficiency would be $2,200/sqft (saleable). You're paying 6.7% more for same usable space.

### Balcony Proportion and Usability

Balconies add strata area but limited usable space. Excessive balconies = poor efficiency.

**Balcony tiers:**
- **Minimal balcony (<10% of strata):** Good efficiency. Typical for urban condos.
- **Standard balcony (10-15% of strata):** Acceptable. Master bedroom + living room balconies typical.
- **Excessive balcony (15-20% of strata):** Poor efficiency. Unit may have 1,050 strata but only 850 saleable.
- **Balcony soup (>20% strata):** Wasteful. Avoid unless heavily discounted.

**Usable balcony assessment:**
- Master balcony: Usually 8-15 sqm. Usable for outdoor seating; valuable.
- Living balcony: Usually 6-12 sqm. Usable for drying, seating; valuable.
- Maid's room balcony: Small (3-6 sqm). Functionally useful but minimal leisure value.
- Bedroom balconies (non-master): Often 3-6 sqm. Limited usability; feels gimmicky.

**Practical assessment:** A 3BR with master + living balconies but no bedroom balconies (total ~25 sqm) = good balcony allocation. A 3BR with master + living + both bedroom balconies (total ~40 sqm) = excessive.

### Bedroom Configuration and Sizes

Bedroom sizes impact both resident comfort and rental appeal.

**Master bedroom standards:**
- Minimum adequate: 4m × 3.5m (allowing queen bed + wardrobe + desk)
- Comfortable: 4.5m × 4m+ (room to spare)
- Small: <3.5m × 3.5m (awkward; bed dominates room)

**Secondary bedroom standards:**
- For young children: 3m × 3m adequate (single bed + shelving)
- For adolescents: 3.5m × 3.5m minimum
- For guests: 3m × 3m acceptable

**Caution:** Some floor plans show "bedrooms" that are actually 2.5m × 2.5m (closet-sized). Verify dimensions on floor plan; ask for actual measurements from sales counter.

**Rental implication:** 3BR units with two equal-sized 3.5m × 3.5m bedrooms (non-master) rent better than 3BR with one large master and two tiny secondaries. Balance appeals to broader tenant pool.

### Household Shelter (Bomb Shelter) Placement

Every unit has a mandatory household shelter (HS). Placement impacts utility:

**Good placement:**
- Separate room (closet-like room), independent access
- Typically in master bedroom or foyer area
- Doesn't eat into main living spaces

**Awkward placement:**
- Within corridor or utility area
- Reduces circulation space usability
- Feels cramped

**Poor placement:**
- Within kitchen or main living room (rare but exists in some layouts)
- Significantly reduces usable living space

**Check:** Review floor plan for HS location. If HS reduces main living space, factor this into layout efficiency assessment.

### Kitchen Type: Enclosed vs Open

**Enclosed kitchen:**
- Traditional; fully separate room
- Advantage: Cooking odors/heat don't spread to living room
- Advantage: Neat appearance (cooking hidden)
- Disadvantage: Less integrated; family feels separated
- Typical for: Family-oriented projects, Asian demographics

**Open kitchen (open to living/dining):**
- Modern; integrated with main living space
- Advantage: Spacious feel; family integration
- Advantage: Cooking area feels larger
- Disadvantage: Cooking odors/heat spread to living areas
- Disadvantage: Requires constant tidiness for aesthetic living room
- Typical for: Modern/boutique projects, young professional focus

**Practical consideration:** Check if unit has both wet (with sink, storage) and dry kitchen (separate pantry) or combined. Dual kitchen is superior (wet for cooking, dry for storage/prep); single kitchen = multitasking friction.

### Bathroom Count and Configuration

Bathroom adequacy varies by unit size:

**2BR unit:**
- Minimum: 1.5 bathrooms (master en-suite + 1 shared)
- Ideal: 2 bathrooms (master en-suite + 1 guest)
- Caution: Some 2BR have only 1 bathroom (undesirable; morning queues inevitable)

**3BR unit:**
- Minimum: 2 bathrooms (master en-suite + 1 shared for 2 bedrooms)
- Ideal: 2.5+ bathrooms (master en-suite + guest + second en-suite)
- Caution: 3BR with only 1 shared bathroom is poor; suggests layout inefficiency

**4BR+ unit:**
- Minimum: 2.5 bathrooms (all bedrooms should have access without sharing)
- Ideal: 3+ bathrooms (master + secondary en-suites + guest)

**Check:** Review floor plan for bathroom count and en-suite access per bedroom. Limited bathroom sharing reduces resident friction and improves rental appeal.

### Natural Ventilation and Cross-Ventilation

Natural ventilation reduces air-con dependency; improves occupant well-being.

**Excellent cross-ventilation:**
- Windows on opposite sides (north/south or east/west)
- Through-units with windows on both sides
- Air flows naturally; air-con usage lower

**Limited ventilation:**
- Windows on one side only
- Dead zones where air stagnates
- Higher air-con dependency

**Check:** Ask "Is this a through-unit or corner-unit with dual exposure?" Units with single-side exposure feel stuffier and cost more to cool.

## New Launch Stack Selection Strategy

Buying a new launch on floor plan requires strategic thinking about phase timing, pricing, and unit selection.

### Phase and Timing Strategy

New launches typically sell in phases. Earlier phases often have better pricing; later phases have better unit selection.

**Phase 1 (Launch):**
- Advantage: Lowest pricing (developer offers launch incentives)
- Advantage: Best unit selection (first pick of floor plans)
- Disadvantage: Uncertainty on final finishes, landscaping completion
- Strategy: Good for investors seeking maximum discount; acceptable for owner-occupants willing to wait for completion

**Phase 2 (Mid-cycle):**
- Advantage: Moderate pricing; Phase 1 sales data justifies adjustments
- Advantage: Some Phase 1 units completed; can inspect finishes
- Disadvantage: Pricing creeping up; best units may be gone
- Strategy: Sweet spot for many buyers; balance of discount and certainty

**Phase 3+ (Late-cycle):**
- Advantage: Actual Phase 1 units completed and handed over; can inspect finishes, visit completed units
- Advantage: Know landscaping, amenity finishes
- Disadvantage: Highest pricing (developer adjusts upward as project sells well)
- Disadvantage: Best stacks likely sold out
- Strategy: Acceptable for buyer-occupants prioritising certainty over discount; avoid for investors

### Ballot Strategy: Which Units to Ballot For

Some new launches use ballots to allocate units if oversubscribed. Strategic ballot placement maximizes optionality.

**Framework:**
1. Identify 3-5 preferred stacks based on facing, floor level, view, noise
2. List each stack from highest to lowest preference
3. Ballot in priority order

**Example ballot plan (3BR focus, Novena new launch):**
- 1st choice: Stack B, levels 15-20, south-facing (open view, good light, high floor)
- 2nd choice: Stack C, levels 12-18, east-facing (morning light, view, quieter)
- 3rd choice: Stack A, levels 10-15, north-facing (no noise, cool, accessible)

**Backup strategy:**
- If 3BR oversubscribed, have 2BR plan (Stack D, corner, south-facing)
- If your stacks all unavailable, pre-identify fallback stacks before ballot results

### Price List Analysis: Finding Value Stacks

Developers publish tiered price lists reflecting unit characteristics. Identifying value outliers maximizes return.

**Methodology:**
1. Get full price list from sales counter (lists all units and prices)
2. Filter by unit type (e.g., all 3BR)
3. Calculate PSF for each unit
4. Identify outliers (units with lower PSF despite similar characteristics)

**Example:**
- Price list shows most 3BR units: $1.65M-$1.8M range
- Unit 15-03 (mid-level, south-facing): $1.72M = $1,638/sqft
- Unit 12-05 (lower level, north-facing): $1.58M = $1,505/sqft (value outlier)
- Unit 28-02 (high level, corner, south): $1.88M = $1,790/sqft (premium outlier)

**Assessment:** Unit 12-05 is trading at $133/sqft (8%) discount. If north-facing isn't a dealbreaker, this is value. Conversely, Unit 28-02 asks heavy premium; validate if justified by location.

**Buy the value outliers.** Don't anchor on the listing price of units without considering relative positioning within price list.
