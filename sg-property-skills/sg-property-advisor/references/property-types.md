# Singapore Property Types Reference

---

## HDB (Housing and Development Board)

HDB flats represent ~80% of Singapore's residential stock. They are the entry point for most first-time buyers and a staple of the Singapore housing ecosystem.

### BTO (Build-to-Order)

**What it is:**
New HDB flats sold by ballot, typically 3–5 years before completion. You vote in a draw; if successful, you book a unit, pay a booking fee, and wait for TOP (Temporary Occupation Permit).

**Timeline:**
1. Ballot launched (competitive draw)
2. Booking fee paid ($500–$1,000)
3. Design revealed, unit specifications shown
4. Progressive payment plan begins (~10% annually until TOP)
5. TOP issued (~3–5 years after ballot)
6. Full occupation key handed over

**Pricing:**
- Set by HDB, typically 10–20% below market resale prices
- Targeted at first-time buyers; income ceiling applies ($14,000/month)

**Pros:**
- Significant price discount vs resale (often $50,000–$150,000 cheaper for same size/location)
- New condition; no defect issues
- Longer payment timeline eases cash flow
- Stable community (new town planning)

**Cons:**
- **Ballot odds are poor** (60–75% of applicants unsuccessful in recent ballots)
- Long wait time (3–5 years) locks you into a future decision; life circumstances change
- Must complete TOP purchase or forfeit booking fee; no exit clause
- **You cannot sell before MOP (5 years).** If you need to move before 5 years, you are in breach and face legal/CPF complications
- Neighbourhood not yet developed; shops, schools, transport may still be in construction phase

**Who it suits:**
- First-time buyers with patience and stability (not expecting major life changes in 5+ years)
- Couples with long timelines before needing to upgrade
- Buyers willing to accept the neighbourhood risk (undeveloped area)

**Critical gotcha:**
Many couples apply for BTO assuming they will upgrade to private property after MOP ends (5 years post-TOP, so 8–10 years total). This is viable but requires discipline — interest rates, income, and market conditions will have changed; stress-test the upgrade affordability now.

### Resale HDB

**What it is:**
Existing HDB flats sold by the current owner. You find, negotiate, and complete within 8–12 weeks. Resale price is market-determined, not HDB-fixed.

**MOP implications:**
- Current owner must have held for ≥5 years from purchase completion
- You cannot check MOP status directly; your lawyer confirms before proceeding
- If MOP not met, sale cannot complete; common pitfall for unwary buyers

**Valuation mechanics:**
- Bank values the property (independent assessment)
- CPF can be withdrawn only up to the bank's valuation, not the purchase price
- If you negotiate a $400,000 purchase price but bank values at $380,000, CPF withdrawal capped at $380,000; you pay $20,000 cash extra for the gap

**Resale grant eligibility (if applicable):**
- Buyers with household income ≤ $14,000/month may be eligible for a **resale flat subsidy/grant** on top of their own CPF
- Grant typically $30,000–$80,000 depending on location, income, and flat type
- Requires application; not automatic

**Price dynamics:**
- Resale prices are market-sensitive; they track the URA Private Residential Property Index
- Floor-level flats, units near MRT, and mature estates command premiums
- Older flats (50+ years) see steeper declines in value

**Pros:**
- Immediate occupancy (no waiting 3–5 years)
- Neighbourhood established; schools, transport, amenities known
- Can inspect the flat before purchase (avoid hidden defects)
- If eligible, resale grant reduces out-of-pocket cost
- Flexibility to buy when you are ready

**Cons:**
- No price subsidy (pay market rate)
- Older flats may have maintenance issues (roof leaks, structural cracks, etc.)
- Selling costs (agent commission, seller's costs) already factored into price
- Longer search period; no guarantee of finding what you want quickly
- MOP breach risk if you buy from an ineligible seller (rare but possible if buyer misses the fact that seller bought and is selling within 5 years; your lawyer should check)

**Who it suits:**
- Buyers with no patience for BTO ballots
- Upgraders moving from one HDB to another (more established neighbourhood preferred)
- First-time buyers with flexible timeline who want to inspect before buying

### DBSS (Design, Build and Sell Scheme)

**What it is:**
HDB-issued licences to private developers to build HDB-like flats with some private design flexibility. These are HDB flats sold by developers, not by HDB directly. They sit between HDB resale and EC in terms of price and features.

**Typical features:**
- Private developers manage design (often more spacious layouts, better finishes than standard HDB)
- Units sold at competitive prices (higher than BTO, lower than EC)
- Still subject to HDB rules (MOP, income ceiling, etc.)

**Timeline:**
Similar to new launch private property: design phase → pre-booking → construction → TOP

**Pros:**
- Newer construction (1–3 years younger than comparable resale HDB)
- Better design and finishes
- Price midway between BTO and EC

**Cons:**
- Still subject to 5-year MOP
- Developer controls timing; delays possible
- Less established "HDB" brand; resale may be slower if developer reputation unclear

**Who it suits:**
- Buyers seeking newer HDB-like units without waiting for BTO ballot
- Those wanting private developer quality at subsidised HDB pricing

## EC (Executive Condominium)

EC sits between HDB and private property. It is a government-assisted housing scheme offering apartment-style ownership with some condo amenities, at a subsidised price. After 10 years, it privatises and becomes private property.

### New EC Launch

**Eligibility:**
- Singapore citizen or PR couple (or single citizen 35+)
- Household income ≤ $14,000/month
- First-time HDB/EC buyer (or upgrading from HDB)

**Pricing:**
- Subsidised by HDB; typically 20–30% below comparable private condo prices
- For example, $600,000–$700,000 for a 2-bedroom vs $800,000–$1,000,000 in private

**MOP:**
- 5 years from purchase completion

**Loan & LTV:**
- LTV 85% for 1st EC loan (vs 75% for 1st private)
- MSR 30% applies (same as HDB)
- TDSR 55% applies (same as all properties)

**Pros:**
- Significant price discount vs private property (most compelling advantage)
- Condo facilities (pool, gym, BBQ area) included
- After 10 years, can privatise and sell freely (MOP ends; becomes private property)
- Newer units with better finishes than HDB

**Cons:**
- **5-year MOP:** Cannot sell or rent out before completion of 5 years; massive constraint
- Income ceiling: Cannot buy if household income > $14,000/month
- Privatisation at year 10 may not occur if < 80% of residents vote to do so (rare, but blocks private sale rights)
- Resale liquidity: Buyers with income above $14,000/month cannot buy resale EC before privatisation, so resale pool is smaller
- Maintenance fees: Typically higher than HDB, lower than private condo

**Privatisation mechanics (Year 10+):**
- At 10 years from purchase, eligible for en bloc sale and privatisation
- If 80%+ residents vote to privatise, the estate converts to private property
- After privatisation, MOP ends; unit can be freely sold
- LTV rules change to private rules (75% → 65% → 60%)
- No income ceiling; anyone can buy resale EC post-privatisation

### Resale EC (Before Privatisation)

**Eligibility to buy:**
- Must still meet income ceiling ($14,000/month)
- Must still meet first-time buyer or upgrader criteria
- Remaining MOP applies (e.g., if original buyer bought 3 years ago, 2 years MOP remains for next buyer)

**Resale price dynamics:**
- Resale EC prices track upward from launch price due to market appreciation
- But premium limited by income ceiling constraint (reduces buyer pool)
- Typically 10–20% appreciation from launch price by year 5

**Pros:**
- Established condo; amenities tested, community known
- Same subsidised nature as new EC if bought early in holding period

**Cons:**
- Income ceiling still applies; less liquid than post-privatisation
- Remaining MOP can be substantial (e.g., 4 years remaining if current owner bought 1 year ago)
- Price premium vs new launch but no further pricing subsidy

### Resale EC (After Privatisation)

**What changes:**
- MOP ends; can be freely sold
- No income ceiling; private buyers can now purchase
- LTV rules flip to private (75% for 1st buyer)
- Treated as private property for all regulatory purposes

**Pricing:**
- Jumps significantly post-privatisation due to removal of income ceiling (expanded buyer pool)
- Typically 10–30% appreciation within 1–2 years of privatisation date
- Unit becomes "unlocked" to all buyers; price aligns closer to comparable private condos

**Pros:**
- Massively increased resale value and liquidity post-privatisation
- Original buyers who weathered 10-year MOP typically see strong appreciation
- Condo amenities and newer construction vs older private properties

**Cons:**
- 10-year wait before this upside is realised
- Cannot freely buy/sell for first 10 years

**Strategic consideration:**
EC is attractive as a 10-year hold, with the exit plan being to privatise, cash in on the appreciation, and upgrade to premium private property. However, 10 years is a long lock-in; family circumstances change. Model this carefully.

## Private Residential Property

### New Launch

**What it is:**
Developer-built apartment buildings, condominiums, or landed properties at launch, sold off-plan or with show units.

**Typical transaction flow:**
1. Showflat viewing; unit reservation with show agent
2. Booking fee (typically 1–3% of price, refundable if you withdraw within a set period)
3. Options to Purchase (OTP) issued; buyer has 3 weeks to exercise (legally commit)
4. Exercise OTP + pay option fee (typically 5% of price, becomes part of down payment)
5. Completion 8–12 weeks later (sale & purchase agreement signed)
6. Progressive payment schedule: 20–30% upon exercise, 40–50% upon completion, 20% upon TOP
7. TOP issued (typically 3–4 years after launch)
8. Keys handed over; renovation and occupation begin
9. Defects Liability Period: 12 months post-TOP; developer fixes minor defects

**Developer pricing strategy:**
Developers often "discount" new launches relative to the open market to build momentum. First 50–100 units may be offered 5–15% below eventual market price. This creates pricing tiers:
- Early bird: $600,000
- Standard launch: $630,000
- Later phases: $660,000
- Market (resale after TOP): $700,000+

**Financing:**
- LTV 75% for 1st private purchase (so 25% down payment required)
- Progressive payments mean you do not need the full 25% upfront (e.g., pay 10% at booking, 15% spread across construction phase)
- This makes new launches more accessible than it appears

**Pros:**
- Builder's warranty (defects liability, structural guarantee)
- Can negotiate price (especially early-bird lots)
- New finishes; no hidden defects
- Flexible progressive payment fits cash flow
- Often includes furnished kitchen, air con, or other perks

**Cons:**
- Completion risk (developer delays, defects, abandonment in rare cases)
- Cannot inspect before purchase (unless show unit is exact replica, typically not true)
- Often 3–4 year wait for TOP; cannot occupy before that
- Prices often reset upward in later phases; early-bird buyers benefit, later buyers overpay
- Neighbourhood may not be fully developed (mixed-use in construction phase)

**Who it suits:**
- Investors (hold for 3–5 years post-TOP for appreciation)
- Buyers with long timelines and patient capital
- Those seeking newest construction and warranty

### Resale Private

**What it is:**
Existing apartments, condos, or landed properties sold by the current owner. Purchased through agents, negotiated, and completed 8–12 weeks later.

**Typical transaction flow:**
1. Viewing (inspect unit directly)
2. Offer made (typically 3–5% below asking price to test seller's resolve)
3. Negotiation; price agreed
4. OTP signed; buyer pays 1% option fee (becomes down payment)
5. 3-week exercise period; buyer finalises financing, loan approval
6. Exercise OTP; commit to purchase
7. Completion 8–12 weeks later; remaining down payment + closing costs paid; keys handed over

**Financing:**
- LTV 75% for 1st private purchase
- Some banks offer 80% LTV in strong market conditions (rare now)

**Price dynamics:**
- Determined by market; if building/unit is in high demand, resale premium emerges
- Comparable resale units in same building set the price floor
- Condo maintenance costs, remaining lease (if leasehold), building age drive price differences

**Lease decay (99-year leasehold):**
A critical factor for Singapore private property. Most private property in Singapore is 99-year leasehold; some is freehold.

**Freehold property:**
- No time limit; ownership perpetual
- Maintains value longer; typically more expensive than leasehold
- Land forever appreciates or stays stable; less depreciation over time
- Example: $2 million freehold condo typically holds value or appreciates

**99-year leasehold property:**
- 99 years from purchase; at year 99, lease expires, and ownership reverts to the state
- As lease shortens (approaching 30 years remaining), property value decays sharply
- At 30 years remaining, most banks will not lend; refinancing becomes impossible
- At 20 years remaining, property becomes difficult to sell except at steep discount
- Typical decay pattern: minimal decay years 1–50, 10–15% decay years 50–70, 30%+ decay years 70–90

**Resale valuation mechanics:**
- Bank values based on comparable sales
- If unit has been heavily renovated, valuation may be closer to asking price
- If unit is dated or in poor condition, bank may value 5–10% below asking price
- CPF withdrawal capped at bank valuation (not asking price)

**Renovation/defects in resale:**
- You must budget for renovation post-purchase (typically $50,000–$150,000 for a 3-bedroom)
- Defects are negotiated as "as is"; seller typically fixes nothing post-completion
- Inspections recommended (professional survey, defect assessment); not mandatory but prudent

**Pros:**
- Immediate occupancy
- Can inspect before purchase (see exact unit, condition, views, noise, etc.)
- Market price transparency (comparables available)
- Flexible buying (no mandatory timeline; can wait for ideal unit)
- Established neighbourhood; amenities, transport, community known

**Cons:**
- Prices reflect full market value; no subsidies
- Agent commission (typically 1–1.5% split between agent and seller) factored into price
- Potential hidden defects (older buildings)
- Longer search; no guarantee of finding ideal unit soon
- Lease decay risk if leasehold unit with <50 years remaining

**Who it suits:**
- Upgraders moving from HDB/EC to private
- Investors seeking established assets with known yields
- Buyers wanting immediate occupancy and verified condition

### Freehold vs 99-Year Leasehold: Long-Term Implications

| Factor | Freehold | 99-Year Leasehold |
|---|---|---|
| **Price premium** | 10–20% higher than equivalent leasehold | Baseline |
| **Appreciation curve** | Stable; minimal decay over 30+ years | Steep decay after year 70; becomes valueless near year 99 |
| **Mortgageability at 30-year lease remaining** | N/A | Banks refuse to lend; refinancing impossible |
| **Refinancing age 70** | Still refinanceable | Problematic; few banks will touch |
| **Investor preference** | Higher; holds value longer | Lower; decay creates exit urgency |
| **Holding horizon** | 30+ years; no urgency to sell | <50 years; must exit before severe decay |
| **Estate planning** | Can pass to heirs indefinitely | Lease expires; heirs inherit diminishing asset |

**Strategic implication:** For buyers with 25+ year horizons, freehold premium is worth paying. For buyers planning to sell within 15 years, leasehold decay is immaterial; leasehold offers better value.

## Landed Property (Houses, Villas, Terrace Houses)

### Ownership & Restrictions

**Who can own:**
- Singapore citizens: Yes
- PRs: Yes
- Foreigners: **No** (except in Sentosa and some specific zones; standard rule is foreigners cannot buy landed property)

**Types:**
- Detached houses
- Semi-detached houses
- Terrace houses (single, double-storey)
- Bungalows
- Villas (luxury landed)

### Leasehold vs Freehold in Landed

**99-year leasehold landed:**
- More common; more affordable
- Lease decay applies (same as apartment leasehold)
- At 30 years remaining, becomes difficult to sell/mortgage

**Freehold landed:**
- Rare; premium pricing (typically 20–30% above equivalent leasehold)
- No lease decay; perpetual ownership
- Higher long-term value retention

### Pricing Dynamics

Landed property prices driven by:
- **Land size:** Larger plots command exponential premiums (e.g., 3,000 sqft vs 2,000 sqft can be 30% price difference)
- **Location:** Landed in prime districts (D10, D15, Farrer Road area) command $15,000–$30,000/sqft; outer areas $5,000–$10,000/sqft
- **Age of structure:** Older houses often bought for land redevelopment (sold at land value, not structure value)
- **Tenure:** Freehold premium substantial; leasehold with <30 years remaining difficult to sell

### Pros
- Land ownership (psychological appeal)
- Space (no shared facilities to queue for)
- Privacy (no neighbours directly above/below)
- Potential for redevelopment (demolish and rebuild or upgrade)

### Cons
- Maintenance burden (roof repairs, garden, structural issues all on you)
- Property tax higher than apartments
- Mortgage rates often 0.25–0.5% higher than apartments
- Leasehold decay issue (30-year threshold more critical for landed because land is the asset)
- Resale slower than apartments (smaller buyer pool)
- Renovation costs high (full house renovation $200,000–$500,000+)

### Who it Suits
- Families wanting space and privacy
- Long-term holders (25+ years) comfortable with maintenance
- Buyers seeking freehold stability
- Investors/developers planning land banking or redevelopment

## Comparison Matrix: Which Property Type?

| Scenario | Best Choice | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **First-time buyer, <$10k income, patient for 3–5 years** | BTO HDB | Lowest cost; price discount; patient timeline works |
| **First-time buyer, <$10k income, need immediate occupancy** | Resale HDB | Established, no waiting, eligible for resale grant |
| **Upgrader from HDB, <$14k income, want condo amenities** | New EC | Price discount; condo feel; path to privatisation upside |
| **Upgrader from HDB, <$14k income, want private now** | Resale private | Escape income ceiling; better pricing in older, >70-year-lease buildings |
| **High-income couple (>$14k), first-time, want flexibility** | Resale private | No income ceiling; can buy anywhere; immediate occupancy |
| **Investor seeking rental yield** | Resale private in mature estate | Tenants available; lower entry cost; established building |
| **Investor seeking appreciation over 10+ years** | New EC (if eligible) | Privatisation upside at year 10; price locked below market |
| **Buyer with 25+ year horizon, seeking land** | Freehold landed | Lease decay immaterial; land appreciation long-term benefit |
| **Buyer with 10–15 year horizon, budget-conscious** | 99-year leasehold resale | Decay immaterial over timeline; cheaper than freehold; easier to exit |

## Holding Period & Exit Considerations

### HDB
- **Minimum holding:** 5 years MOP (non-negotiable)
- **Typical holding:** 7–15 years (covers BTO wait + MOP + some appreciation)
- **Exit strategy:** Upgrade to EC or private after MOP (5 years post-completion)

### EC
- **Minimum holding:** 5 years MOP (non-negotiable)
- **Holding period for upside:** 10 years (to privatisation + appreciation)
- **Typical holding:** 10–20 years
- **Exit strategy:** At year 10, wait for privatisation; sell at inflated price to private buyer pool

### Private (Resale, <30 years lease)
- **Minimum holding:** 3 years (to avoid SSD entirely; 1 year = 12% tax, 2 years = 8% tax)
- **Typical holding:** 5–10 years
- **Exit strategy:** Sell during market peak; reinvest in stronger asset or downsize

### Private (Freehold or new <30 years lease)
- **Minimum holding:** 3 years (SSD perspective)
- **Typical holding:** 10+ years (lease decay immaterial)
- **Exit strategy:** Hold longer; lease decay not a concern for decades

## Summary: Property Type Decision Tree

**Start here:**
1. **What is your household income?**
   - ≤ $14,000/month: HDB, EC, or private (no income ceiling for private)
   - > $14,000/month: Private only (no HDB/EC new launch access)

2. **How soon do you need to move in?**
   - Now: Resale HDB or resale private
   - 3–5 years: BTO HDB or new launch private
   - 5+ years: EC (privatisation play) or new launch private

3. **How much can you invest (cash + borrowing)?**
   - Up to $500k: HDB or lower-end EC/private
   - $500k–$1.5m: EC, resale private in established estates
   - > $1.5m: New launch private, freehold landed

4. **What is your holding horizon?**
   - 5–7 years: HDB (MOP exit)
   - 7–10 years: EC (privatisation play) or resale private
   - 10+ years: Any; lease decay immaterial, focus on location quality

5. **Are you an investor or owner-occupier?**
   - Owner-occupier: Prioritise livability + appreciation + exit flexibility
   - Investor: Prioritise yield + appreciation potential + tenant demand

Use these factors to narrow to 1–2 options, then engage specialist skills (sg-property-analyser for unit/project analysis, sg-property-calculator for affordability modelling).
