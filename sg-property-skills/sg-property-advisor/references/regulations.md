# Singapore Property Regulations Reference

**Last verified:** 2025-Q4. Regulations change frequently. Always verify current rates with MAS, IRAS, HDB, and your bank before structuring a transaction.

---

## ABSD (Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty)

### Overview
ABSD is a progressive tax on residential property purchases based on buyer type and property count. It applies on top of standard BSD.

### Current Rates (2025)

#### Singapore Citizens
| Property Count | Rate |
|---|---|
| 1st property | 0% |
| 2nd property | 7% |
| 3rd+ property | 12% |

#### Permanent Residents (PR)
| Property Count | Rate |
|---|---|
| 1st property | 5% |
| 2nd property | 10% |
| 3rd+ property | 15% |

#### Foreigners
- Flat 20% on all residential purchases (HDB not eligible)

#### Entities (Companies, Trusts, Syndicates)
- 25% (applies to all company purchases; exceptions for wholly-owned subsidiaries of foreign entities in limited cases)

### Key Rules
- **Property count resets** if more than 6 years elapse between consecutive acquisitions for citizens (pro-rating rules apply)
- **Joint ownership:** ABSD applies if ANY buyer is a PR or foreigner; the rate reflects the highest-tier buyer in the group
- **Resale vs new:** ABSD applies to both
- **HDB and EC:** Citizens exempt (0% ABSD); PRS and foreigners not eligible to own
- **Private property:** All buyer types subject to ABSD rates above
- **Decoupling (private):** If one spouse retains ownership and the other sells, the seller's property count for ABSD purposes depends on whether they retain any residential property after the sale

## BSD (Buyer's Stamp Duty)

### Overview
BSD is a stamp duty on the purchase price of any property. Rates are tiered by purchase price and apply regardless of buyer type (prior to ABSD).

### Current Rates (2025)

| Purchase Price | BSD Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to $180,000 | 1.0% |
| $180,001 – $300,000 | 1.5% |
| $300,001 – $500,000 | 2.0% |
| $500,001 – $750,000 | 3.0% |
| $750,001 – $1,000,000 | 4.0% |
| Above $1,000,000 | 4.5% |

### Calculation
BSD is levied on the **purchase price or market valuation, whichever is higher**. For new launches, developers often apply valuations below asking prices; stamp duty is calculated on the higher amount.

### Who Pays
- **Buyer** in almost all cases
- In rare negotiations, seller may agree to pay buyer's BSD (unusual, requires explicit agreement)

## TDSR (Total Debt Servicing Ratio)

### Overview
TDSR caps the proportion of monthly income that can service all property-related and non-property debt. It is the single biggest constraint on borrowing capacity for most buyers.

### Key Thresholds
- **Maximum TDSR:** 55% of gross monthly income
- **Applies to:** All residential mortgages in Singapore (HDB, EC, private)
- **Enforced by:** MAS; all banks must comply

### Calculation Methodology
**TDSR = (Monthly debt obligations) / (Gross monthly income)**

**Monthly debt obligations include:**
- All property loans (outstanding mortgage instalments across all properties)
- Car loans
- Personal loans
- Credit card liabilities (minimum 5% of outstanding balance)
- Student loans
- Hire purchase agreements
- Rental commitments (if landlord under HDB/EC, income counted as offset to outgoing)

**Gross monthly income:**
- Basic salary + fixed allowances (entertainment, transport, housing)
- Bonus (averaged over past 2 years, capped at base salary)
- Commission/variable income (averaged over past 2 years)
- CPF contribution by employer (included in income)
- For self-employed: average net profit over past 2–3 years
- Rental income (only if property fully rented out)

### Critical Points
- TDSR is **a ceiling, not a target.** Most prudent borrowers aim for 40–45% to allow for income shocks and rate increases.
- **Rate stress-testing:** Banks may apply an assumed rate (e.g., 3.5–4.5%) to calculate TDSR, regardless of current loan rate. Verify your bank's assumption.
- **Relationship changes:** If jointly borrowing, both incomes count. On divorce, only one income remains — TDSR recalculated on remaining income alone.
- **Income verification:** Requires recent payslips (last 2–3 months), tax returns, employment letter. Self-employed need audited accounts.

## MSR (Mortgage Servicing Ratio)

### Overview
MSR caps the proportion of monthly income that can service **property loans only** (not total debt). It is less restrictive than TDSR but still material.

### Thresholds
- **HDB and EC:** 30% of gross monthly income
- **Private property:** Not statutorily capped; banks typically apply 55% TDSR as the constraint instead

### Calculation
**MSR = (Monthly mortgage instalment on all properties) / (Gross monthly income)**

### Application
- **HDB:** Buyers must meet MSR ≤ 30% to qualify for HDB housing loans
- **EC:** Same 30% MSR cap for EC loans
- **Private:** Banks do not separately enforce MSR; TDSR constraint is binding

### Why It Matters
- If you breach HDB MSR (e.g., 32%), you cannot borrow from HDB even if TDSR is acceptable
- Private property buyers only need to satisfy TDSR, which is more flexible

## LTV (Loan-to-Value) Limits

### Overview
LTV limits the loan amount as a percentage of property value (or purchase price, whichever is lower). They vary by buyer type, loan count, and property age/tenure.

### Current LTV Limits (2025)

#### HDB
| Loan Count | LTV Limit |
|---|---|
| 1st HDB loan | 90% |
| 2nd HDB loan | 80% |
| 3rd+ HDB loan | 80% |

**Age & tenure rules:** No change based on age; applies from purchase regardless of buyer age.

#### EC (Executive Condominium)
| Loan Count | LTV Limit |
|---|---|
| 1st EC loan | 85% |
| 2nd EC loan | 75% |
| 3rd+ EC loan | 75% |

**Privatisation impact:** Once EC is privatised (after 10 years and after en bloc sale), it is treated as private property; LTV rules below apply.

#### Private Property
| Loan Count | LTV Limit | Age/Tenure Condition |
|---|---|---|
| 1st private loan | 75% | Purchase any age |
| 2nd private loan | 65% | — |
| 3rd+ private loan | 60% | — |

**Lease decay (99-year leasehold):**
- Leasehold ≤ 30 years remaining: Banks may reduce LTV further (typically to 50–60%)
- At 30-year mark, refinancing becomes difficult; most banks won't lend

**Freehold:** No lease decay; maintains 75%/65%/60% LTV throughout ownership

### Critical Points
- **LTV applies to the lower of purchase price or valuation.** If you negotiate a discount, the valuation (bank's assessment) may be higher than your purchase price; LTV is based on valuation.
- **Loan count includes previous loans not yet fully repaid,** even if property has been sold (for 6 years after sale in some banks' calculations).
- **Joint ownership:** If both spouses buy together, it counts as one loan; if one spouse is removed later, it may trigger loan restructuring.
- **At 75+ years old (with 25-year tenure remaining):** LTV may drop further; most banks will not lend beyond 25-year tenure to borrowers over 75.

## CPF (Central Provident Fund) Usage for Property

### OA (Ordinary Account) Withdrawal

**Eligible purposes:**
- Purchase of HDB, EC, or private property (entire OA available for purchase)
- Repayment of housing loan (monthly instalments)
- Payment of property-related outgoings (annual property tax, insurance, HDB maintenance fees)

**Withdrawal limits:**
- **HDB purchase:** Full OA balance usable (no cap)
- **EC purchase:** Full OA balance usable (no cap)
- **Private property purchase:** CPF used cannot exceed 80% of purchase price; cash down payment must be at least 20%

**Valuation limit (resale):**
- For HDB/EC resale, CPF can be withdrawn only up to the **valuation amount set by the bank or HDB**, not the full purchase price
- If purchase price exceeds valuation, excess must be paid in cash

### Accrued Interest Concept

**What is accrued interest?**
CPF balances earn an implicit interest rate. When you withdraw OA for property purchase, the "accrued interest" (the accumulated gains on your CPF balance from the moment you started CPF contributions) must be repaid when you sell or downsize.

**Calculation:**
- CPF tracks your contributions and deemed interest separately
- On sale of property, your CPF OA is "topped up" to repay the accrued interest owed
- For HDB: CPF receives the nett sale proceeds after agent commission and any seller's costs, and accrued interest is deducted from your return
- For private: CPF receives proceeds directly

**Critical point:** Accrued interest compounds. On a $300,000 HDB purchase using $200,000 CPF, accrued interest may be $40,000–$60,000+ after 10–15 years, depending on holding period and contribution history. This is a real cost often underestimated by first-time sellers.

### CPF at Age 55 and Beyond

**At age 55:**
- You can no longer make new CPF withdrawals for property unless you are repaying an existing loan
- You must maintain a **Basic Retirement Sum (BRS)** in your Retirement Account (RA); excess OA can be used for property

**At age 65:**
- If property is still held, you must set aside a **Full Retirement Sum (FRS)** in RA
- Excess OA can still be withdrawn for property repayment, but the FRS grows

**Selling after 65:**
- Sale proceeds go into your CPF RA (which has the FRS restriction)
- Limited liquidity; most of proceeds are locked for retirement

### CPF Refund on Sale

**Process:**
1. Sale proceeds received (net of costs)
2. CPF deducts the original principal amount withdrawn for the purchase
3. CPF adds accrued interest that was earned on the withdrawn amount (this amount is added to your CPF balance)
4. Any remaining cash proceeds go to you
5. If sale price < CPF withdrawal amount, you must pay the shortfall in cash

**Example:**
- Bought HDB for $400,000 in 2015 using $300,000 CPF + $100,000 cash
- Sold in 2026 for $420,000 (net of costs $390,000)
- CPF refund: $300,000 original withdrawal + $25,000 accrued interest = $325,000 credited to CPF
- Cash to you: $390,000 − $325,000 = $65,000
- This is why CPF refunds often surprise sellers; accrued interest is substantial

## MOP (Minimum Occupation Period)

### HDB MOP
- **Standard:** 5 years from completion of purchase
- **BTO:** MOP starts from date of TOP (Temporary Occupation Permit), not initial booking
- **Resale:** MOP starts from date of purchase completion (HDB resale application date)
- **Consequence:** Cannot sell, rent out, or transfer before MOP ends; breach triggers legal liability and CPF complications

### EC (Executive Condominium) MOP
- **Standard:** 5 years from date of purchase completion
- **DBSS (Design, Build and Sell Scheme):** 5 years MOP applies; DBSS units are often lower-priced alternatives with same MOP burden
- **Privatisation:** After 10 years from purchase, EC becomes private property; previous MOP rules end, and unit can be freely transacted

### Private Property
- **No MOP.** Can sell or rent out immediately after purchase (subject to SSD rules if within 3 years)

## SSD (Seller's Stamp Duty) – Private Property Only

**Applies to private residential property only. HDB and EC have no SSD.**

### Current Rates (2025)

| Holding Period | SSD Rate |
|---|---|
| Within 1 year | 12% of sale price |
| Year 1–2 (between 1 and 2 years) | 8% of sale price |
| Year 2–3 (between 2 and 3 years) | 4% of sale price |
| 3+ years | Nil |

### Calculation
SSD is levied on the **sale price or market valuation, whichever is higher**.

### Critical Points
- **Holding period:** Calculated from purchase completion date to sale completion date
- **Married couple selling jointly:** If only one spouse held the property, SSD calculated on their portion of sale proceeds
- **Flip protection:** SSD effectively prevents profitable short-term flipping; most private purchases are held 3+ years to avoid SSD
- **Impact on ROI:** A property bought at $600,000 and sold at $650,000 after 18 months triggers 8% SSD ($52,000), wiping out much of the gain

## Decoupling – Private Property Only

### Overview
Decoupling is the process of separating joint ownership of a private property into individual ownership. One spouse retains; one spouse transfers their share to the other or sells their share.

### Common Scenario
Couple owns a private property jointly. They later decide one spouse will hold it, the other will be free to buy another property without ABSD implications (as a "first-time buyer" again).

### Mechanics

**Option 1: Transfer of share (within the couple)**
- Spouse A transfers their 50% share to Spouse B
- **Stamp duty:** Conveyance duty applies on the market value of the 50% transferred (not on explicit payment if undervalued)
- **ABSD:** The transferor (spouse giving up the share) is treated as if they no longer own this property for ABSD purposes; their next purchase counts as "1st property" if they do not own another residential property
- **BSD:** Applied only on the transferor's share of the property value
- **Timing:** Transfer typically done before the exiting spouse buys their next property, so they can claim "1st property" ABSD rate

**Option 2: One spouse sells their share (to the other or to a third party)**
- Same outcome: selling spouse is free to buy again as a "1st-time buyer"
- Buyer of the share pays ABSD based on property count

### ABSD Implications

**Critical:** ABSD changes depending on **property count at the time of next purchase**.

**Scenario 1 (Decoupling via transfer to keep property in family):**
- Spouse A transfers 50% to Spouse B → Spouse A now owns 0 properties
- Spouse A buys a new property → Counts as 1st property (0% ABSD for citizens)
- Spouse B now owns the original property + their new property = 2nd property for ABSD purposes

**Scenario 2 (Clean separation):**
- Spouse A transfers 50% to Spouse B → Spouse A owns 0 properties
- Spouse A buys a new property, Spouse B keeps original property
- Each can apply different ABSD rates depending on their individual property count

### BSD Impact During Decoupling
The share transfer triggers stamp duty on the valuation of the transferred portion. If original property valued at $800,000, transferring 50% to spouse incurs stamp duty on $400,000 valuation.

### Timing Considerations
- Decoupling **before** the exiting spouse buys their next property is essential; otherwise, they are in a "holding" period where they own two properties (old + new) and face higher ABSD on the new purchase
- Lawyers typically advise completing decoupling 1–2 weeks before the next purchase to ensure clean property count documentation

## Trust Structures & Nominee Arrangements

### Trust Ownership of Property

**Structure:**
Property is held by a trustee on behalf of beneficiaries (often spouses or family members). Legally, the trustee owns; beneficiaries have equitable interest.

**Advantages:**
- Flexibility in ownership transfer (trustee can change beneficiary designations without selling property)
- Estate planning (simplifies succession planning)
- Potential privacy (property not directly registered to individual)

**Disadvantages:**
- ABSD and BSD still apply based on trustee's property count (not individual beneficiary count)
- Banks and HDB often scrutinise trust structures; some will not lend on trust property
- Stamp duty on trust deed creation and modification
- Ongoing compliance and trust accounting costs

**HDB and EC:** Cannot be held in trust; must be in individual names

**Private property:** Can be held in trust; increasingly common for high-value properties and family planning

### Nominee Arrangements

**Definition:**
A named owner (nominee) holds legal title on behalf of a beneficial owner (often spouse). Beneficial owner has equitable interest but is not on the title.

**Why used (increasingly rare due to regulatory scrutiny):**
- Bypass ABSD restrictions (though this is now considered circumvention and is actively discouraged by regulators)
- Avoid MOP implications (for HDB, EC — though this too is scrutinised)

**CRITICAL:** Using nominees to evade ABSD or MOP is considered fraud. IRAS and HDB actively investigate suspicious nominee arrangements. Modern practice strongly discourages this approach.

**Current regulatory stance:** Nominee arrangements are permissible for legitimate purposes (e.g., asset protection, family structures) but must be disclosed and cannot be used to circumvent taxation or MOP rules.

## Eligibility Criteria

### HDB Purchase

**Citizenship requirement:**
- At least one applicant must be a Singapore citizen
- PR spouse cannot be sole applicant but can be co-applicant

**Age requirement:**
- Minimum 21 years old
- Single applicants must be 35 years old (applies to never-married, divorced, or widowed individuals) to purchase HDB

**Occupation requirement:**
- Employed, self-employed, or on valid work permit
- Housewives can be included if spouse is employed (not primary applicant typically)

**Ownership history:**
- First-time buyer: No prior ownership of HDB in last 30 months (SSD applies: cannot own two HDB within 30 months)
- Upgraders: Can own resale HDB while buying BTO if meeting eligibility

**Income ceiling (2025):**
- **BTO:** $14,000/month combined household gross income
- **Resale HDB:** $14,000/month combined household gross income
- Exceed ceiling → not eligible for HDB, must buy private property

### EC (Executive Condominium) Purchase

**Citizenship requirement:**
- At least one applicant must be a Singapore citizen
- PR spouse can be co-applicant

**Age requirement:**
- Minimum 21 years old
- Single applicants must be 35 years old (same as HDB)

**Income ceiling (2025):**
- **New EC (Launch):** $14,000/month combined household gross income
- **Resale EC (before privatisation):** $14,000/month combined household gross income
- After privatisation (10+ years): No income ceiling; treated as private property

**Ownership history:**
- **1st EC:** No prior ownership of HDB or EC in last 30 months
- **Subsequent ownership:** Similar restrictions apply (cannot own two HDB/EC within 30 months)

**Special rule:** BTO application while owning resale HDB is possible if not breaching the 30-month rule or other eligibility

### Private Property Purchase

**Citizenship requirement:**
- Singapore citizens and PRs can buy
- Foreigners can buy (subject to ABSD 20%, some local property restrictions)
- Entities (companies, trusts) can buy (subject to ABSD 25%)

**Age requirement:**
- Minimum 18 years old (with parental consent)
- Most lenders require 21+

**Occupation requirement:**
- Generally none; investment/foreign buyers can purchase

**Ownership history:**
- No MOP; can sell immediately
- **Foreigners:** Cannot buy landed property (houses, villas) in Singapore; only apartments/condominiums

**Restricted properties:**
- Landed property in Tiong Bahru, Bukit Timah, Tanglin, Holland Road area (Farrer Road-Mount Pleasant area) — foreigners restricted
- Some Sentosa/island properties have restrictions

## Income Ceiling Implications

### BTO and EC Income Ceiling

**Threshold:** $14,000/month combined household income

**Impact if exceeded:**
- Ineligible for BTO launch
- Ineligible for new EC launch
- **Can still buy resale HDB or resale EC** (but resale EC must be before privatisation)
- Must buy private property if seeking to own new HDB/EC

### Strategic Planning

High-income couples ($15,000+) often face a dilemma:
- Cannot access BTO pricing (typical price: $400,000–$550,000 for 4-room)
- Must buy private ($800,000–$2,000,000+ for comparable size)
- OR: Buy resale HDB if available (no income ceiling for resale)

**Common approach:** Buy resale HDB to access affordability, then upgrade to EC/private after 5 years MOP expires

## Key Calculation Examples

### TDSR Calculation
**Scenario:** Buyer earning $6,000/month, existing car loan $800/month, buying HDB with mortgage $2,500/month

**TDSR = ($800 + $2,500) / $6,000 = 55%**
✓ Acceptable (exactly at ceiling; no margin for error)

**With 0.5% rate increase → mortgage rises to $2,650**
**TDSR = ($800 + $2,650) / $6,000 = 57.5%**
✗ Breaches ceiling; bank will not lend

### ABSD Calculation
**Scenario 1:** Singapore citizen, 2nd property purchase, $800,000 private property

**ABSD = 7% of $800,000 = $56,000**
**BSD = 3% of $800,000 = $24,000**
**Total stamp duty = $56,000 + $24,000 = $80,000 (10% of price)**

**Scenario 2:** PR, 1st property purchase, same $800,000 property

**ABSD = 5% of $800,000 = $40,000**
**BSD = 3% of $800,000 = $24,000**
**Total stamp duty = $40,000 + $24,000 = $64,000 (8% of price)**

### LTV and Loan Quantum
**Scenario:** 1st private property purchase, $500,000 price, 75% LTV limit

**Max loan = $500,000 × 75% = $375,000**
**Min down payment = $125,000 (25%)**

If bank valuation = $480,000 (discount to market):
**Max loan = $480,000 × 75% = $360,000**
**Actual down payment = $140,000 (28%)**

## Warnings & Critical Notes

1. **Rates change.** ABSD, BSD, TDSR, and MSR are periodically adjusted. Always verify current rates with MAS, IRAS, or your mortgage bank before structuring a transaction.

2. **Bank discretion.** While regulatory ceilings are stated above, individual banks may apply stricter rules (lower LTV, higher rate assumptions for TDSR). Always confirm with your bank before committing.

3. **CPF accrued interest is real.** Many first-time sellers are shocked by the size of the CPF refund deduction. Model this carefully in your exit strategy.

4. **MOP breach is expensive.** Selling before MOP ends can trigger legal consequences and CPF complications. Never underestimate the 5-year lock-in.

5. **Decoupling has timing requirements.** If done incorrectly (e.g., one spouse buys second property while still on title of first), ABSD implications are severe. Involve your lawyer early.

6. **Stamp duty on higher of price vs valuation.** Banks often value new launches lower than asking price; you pay stamp duty on the higher amount. Budget accordingly.
