# Transaction Playbook: Singapore Property Transactions

---

## Key Principle: The Binding Commitment Point

**Understanding when you are legally committed to buy is CRITICAL.**

In Singapore property, there are three phases:
1. **Pre-commitment:** Viewing, negotiation, enquiry (can walk away freely)
2. **Binding commitment:** Signing LOO (Letter of Offer, HDB) or exercising OTP (private), or signing resale HDB agreement
3. **Post-commitment:** Completion (point of no return; funds exchanged, keys transferred)

**Many buyers think they are committed earlier than they actually are.** Understanding the exact moment of binding commitment prevents costly mistakes.

## Buying New Launch Private Property

### Timeline Overview
- Viewing & negotiation: 1–4 weeks
- Booking fee & OTP: 1 week
- OTP exercise period: 3 weeks (legally binding exercise window)
- Completion: 8–12 weeks after exercise
- TOP & keys: 3–4 years after completion (for residential)

### Step-by-Step Process

#### Phase 1: Showflat Viewing & Unit Selection (Weeks 1–4)

**What happens:**
- Visit showflat (often not your exact unit; may be similar layout/finishes)
- Agent shows unit location on plan (often unbuilt; visualisation only)
- Discuss unit specifications, pricing, available units
- Negotiate price (first 50–100 units often have early-bird discounts; negotiate for premium)

**Key documents:**
- Brochure (architectural plans, facility list, pricing guide)
- Show unit layout and finishes (note: YOUR unit may differ slightly)
- Developer's track record (check past projects, quality, on-time delivery)

**What you are NOT committed to:**
- Everything at this stage is optional. You can walk away.

**Pitfall:** Assuming show unit = your unit's finishes. Developer often uses premium finishes in show unit; your actual unit may have standard finishes. Read the fine print on what is included in your purchase price.

#### Phase 2: Booking Fee & Unit Reservation (Week 1–2)

**What you do:**
- Pay booking fee (typically 1–3% of purchase price; e.g., $18,000–$24,000 on $600k property)
- Sign booking form (non-binding; can withdraw if developer allows)
- Unit is reserved for you (not yet purchased; still refundable)

**What the booking fee covers:**
- Secures unit for you (agent removes from market)
- Refundable if you change mind (within period specified, typically 7–14 days; check developer's terms)
- Becomes part of your down payment if you proceed (credited to OTP exercise)

**What you are NOT committed to:**
- Booking fee is refundable in most cases (though some developers charge cancellation fee; read terms carefully)

**Pitfall:** Assuming booking fee is non-refundable. Many developers allow withdrawal; ask before paying.

#### Phase 3: Options to Purchase (OTP) Signing (Weeks 2–3)

**What happens:**
- Developer issues OTP (formal offer to sell; contains all terms, price, completion date)
- You review OTP with your lawyer
- You sign OTP; you now have 3 weeks to decide to exercise or not

**OTP contents:**
- Purchase price (fixed; no further negotiation after this)
- Completion date (fixed; typically 8–12 weeks)
- Specifications of unit (floor plan, finishes, appliances)
- Defects liability period (typically 12 months post-TOP)
- Cooling-off period (optional; some developers offer 5-day cooling-off; not mandatory)

**Option fee:**
- Typically 5% of purchase price (e.g., $30,000 on $600k)
- Paid with OTP signing
- Non-refundable; becomes part of down payment
- If you don't exercise OTP within 3 weeks, you lose the option fee (and the unit)

**What you are NOT committed to:**
- Signing OTP does NOT bind you to purchase. You have 3 weeks to decide.
- Within 3 weeks, you can walk away by forfeiting the option fee (you lose $30,000, but avoid the full purchase)

**What IS binding:**
- If you don't exercise OTP within 3 weeks, the offer expires. You cannot come back later.

**Pitfall:** Misunderstanding the 3-week window. This is a hard deadline. Missing it = offer expires, you lose option fee, unit goes back to market.

#### Phase 4: OTP Exercise (Week 3–4)

**What happens:**
- Within 3 weeks of OTP signing, you confirm: "I want to buy this property"
- Submit exercise letter to developer's lawyer
- Pay first tranche of down payment (typically 20% of purchase price after option fee; so you've paid 5% option fee + 20% down = 25% total down payment by now)

**This is the BINDING COMMITMENT point.**

Once OTP is exercised:
- You are legally committed to purchase
- Developer cannot back out
- You cannot back out without legal consequences (lose monies paid + risk damages claim)
- Property is now yours in law (though you don't have keys yet)

**What you must have in place BEFORE exercising:**
- Mortgage pre-approval (bank has confirmed they will lend you the amount)
- Funds for down payment (verified; sitting in your account)
- Lawyer reviewed all terms (search for hidden clauses, unfair terms)

**Pitfall:** Exercising OTP before mortgage pre-approval. If pre-approval falls through after OTP exercise, you are stuck. Some banks pull pre-approval due to credit score drop, job change, etc. Get pre-approval BEFORE exercising.

#### Phase 5: Completion (Weeks 8–12 after Exercise)

**What happens:**
- Sale & Purchase (S&P) agreement signed (formal legal contract; contains completion terms)
- Final 5% of down payment due (if not already paid)
- Remaining mortgage funds (75% of price) transferred to developer
- Keys handed over (though unit is unbuilt; you don't physically get keys until TOP)

**What you must do:**
- Arrange home insurance (required by lender; must be in place before completion)
- Arrange property survey/inspection (if you want; not mandatory; buildings are new, so less critical)
- Transfer CPF to completion account (if using CPF for down payment; bank coordinates timing)

**What happens post-completion:**
- You own the property (legal title in your name)
- Developer now cannot sell it to someone else
- You are liable for property tax, insurance, maintenance fees if applicable
- You cannot occupy until TOP (Temporary Occupation Permit) issued

**Pitfall:** Not arranging insurance. Lender will not release final mortgage funds without proof of insurance. This delays completion.

#### Phase 6: Construction & TOP (Months 5–48)

**Timeline:**
- Construction begins (post-completion)
- Regular inspections by buyer (some developers allow inspections at milestones; check contract)
- TOP issued (typically 3–4 years after completion; sometimes earlier, sometimes delayed)
- Inspection by authorities (fire safety, structural, electrical; passed before TOP)

**TOP (Temporary Occupation Permit):**
- Issued by Building and Construction Authority (BCA)
- Certifies building is safe for occupation
- You can now move into the property
- Keys handed over to you

**Defects Liability Period:**
- Starts from TOP; lasts 12 months
- Developer responsible for fixing minor defects (water leaks, paint issues, defective appliances)
- You have 12 months to report; must be fixed at developer's cost
- Major structural defects (cracks, subsidence) are developer's liability beyond 12 months

**Pitfall:** Not inspecting property post-TOP. Schedule thorough inspection within 12-month defects period; report all issues to developer in writing (email + formal notice). After 12 months, defects are your responsibility.

### Financial Progression (Example: $600k property)

| Phase | Payment | Cumulative | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Booking | $12,000 (2%) | $12,000 | Refundable if cancel before certain date |
| OTP signing | $30,000 (5% option fee) | $42,000 | Non-refundable; deadline 3 weeks to exercise |
| OTP exercise | $108,000 (18%) | $150,000 (25% total) | Binding commitment point |
| Completion | $30,000 (5%) | $180,000 (30%) | Final cash down payment |
| **Total down payment** | | **$180,000 (30%)** | Remaining $420,000 via mortgage (70% LTV for new launch; higher than standard 75%) |

Note: New launch financing is often 70% LTV (30% down), not 75% LTV. Confirm with bank upfront.

## Buying Resale Private Property

### Timeline Overview
- Viewing & offer: 1–8 weeks (market-dependent)
- Offer accepted: Immediate
- OTP signing: 1–2 weeks
- OTP exercise: 3 weeks
- Completion: 8–12 weeks after exercise

### Step-by-Step Process

#### Phase 1: Viewing & Offer (Weeks 1–8)

**What happens:**
- Engage property agent (or view independently if private sale)
- View multiple units; shortlist preferences
- Research comparable prices (check URA caveats, recent sales in same building)
- Make verbal offer (through agent)

**Typical offer strategy:**
- Seller asking price: $600,000
- Your offer: $570,000 (5% below asking; tests seller's resolve)
- Seller counter: $590,000
- You counter: $580,000
- Agreement: $585,000 (split the difference)

**Timeline:**
- In strong market, process fast (1–2 weeks)
- In weak market, negotiation longer (4–8 weeks)
- Agent negotiates on your behalf (you don't meet seller directly, usually)

**What you are NOT committed to:**
- Verbal offer is not binding. Either party can walk away at this stage.

**Pitfall:** Making offer without financing pre-approval. If offer accepted and you cannot secure mortgage, you are in breach (lose earnest money if deal falls through due to your inability to finance). Get pre-approval BEFORE making offer.

#### Phase 2: Offer Accepted (Immediate)

**What happens:**
- Agent confirms seller has accepted your price ($585,000 in example)
- You are expected to proceed (though technically not yet legally bound)
- Buyer's agent requests seller's side to prepare OTP

**What you are NOT committed to:**
- Verbal acceptance is not legally binding in Singapore (despite common belief)
- You can still back away, but losing credibility with agent/seller (and missing opportunity)

**Pitfall:** Assuming verbal acceptance = you are bound. In Singapore, binding contract is OTP signing. However, backing out after verbal acceptance damages reputation and may result in agent refusing to work with you again.

#### Phase 3: OTP Signing (Week 2–3)

**What happens:**
- Agent brings OTP to you (prepared by seller's lawyer, based on standard form)
- You review OTP with YOUR lawyer (critical; don't skip this)
- OTP contains:
  - Property address and details
  - Purchase price ($585,000)
  - Completion date (typically 8–12 weeks later)
  - CPF usage terms (how much CPF will be withdrawn, to which account)
  - Inspection period (typically 5 days; can walk away if major issues found)
  - Any encumbrances (eg. vendor's outstanding mortgage, liens)

**Cooling-off period:**
- Singapore law provides 5-day cooling-off period for resale property (HDB & private)
- After OTP signing, you have 5 days to back out without penalty (return OTP unsigned, essentially)
- After 5 days, you are bound

**Option fee:**
- Typically 1% of purchase price (e.g., $5,850 on $585k)
- Paid with OTP signing
- Non-refundable (becomes credit towards down payment at completion)
- If you invoke cooling-off (within 5 days), you lose option fee

**What IS binding:**
- After 5-day cooling-off period, OTP is binding contract
- You are legally committed to buy
- Seller is legally committed to sell
- Neither party can back out without legal consequences

**What you must verify BEFORE signing OTP:**
1. **Seller's title:** Is seller the legal owner? (Check with agent/lawyer)
2. **Outstanding mortgage:** Does seller still owe mortgage? (Must be discharged at completion)
3. **Encumbrances:** Are there any liens, caveats, or claims on property?
4. **CPF refund mechanics:** How much CPF will be returned to you after refund? (This is often underestimated)
5. **Completion timeline:** Is 8–12 weeks realistic? Can you arrange finances in time?

**Pitfall:** Not invoking cooling-off period when major issues found. You have 5 days to inspect and back out. If you wait 6 days to hire inspector, too late to back out without penalty.

#### Phase 4: Inspection Period (Days 1–5 post-OTP)

**What happens (if you choose to inspect):**
- Hire professional surveyor/inspector ($200–$500 cost; optional but recommended for older buildings)
- Conduct thorough inspection: water leaks, cracks, electrical issues, plumbing, structural
- Obtain inspection report

**Inspection findings:**
- Minor issues (paint, fixtures): Seller typically does not fix; you accept as-is
- Major issues (water damage, structural cracks): You can negotiate price reduction or renegotiate
- Severe issues (foundation damage, structural failure): You can invoke cooling-off and back out without penalty

**Pitfall:** Assuming seller will fix issues. Resale property is "as-is" in most cases. Negotiating price reduction is option; demanding seller fix is usually fruitless.

#### Phase 5: OTP Exercise (After Day 5)

**What happens:**
- 5-day cooling-off period expires
- You formally confirm: "I will complete the purchase"
- OTP is now binding contract (if you didn't back out during cooling-off)

**This is the BINDING COMMITMENT point.**

Once OTP is exercised (or cooling-off period expires without withdrawal):
- You are legally committed
- Cannot back out without losing deposit and facing damages claim
- Seller cannot back out

**What you must have in place BEFORE 5-day period ends:**
- Mortgage pre-approval (bank confirms they will lend)
- Inspection completed (if you want to invoke cooling-off)
- Funds arranged (down payment secured)

**Pitfall:** Assuming inspection is mandatory. It's optional; many buyers skip it and regret post-purchase.

#### Phase 6: Completion (Weeks 8–12 after OTP)

**What happens:**
- Seller's lawyer discharges outstanding mortgage (using sale proceeds)
- Your lawyer coordinates with lender and CPF board
- Down payment + mortgage funds transferred
- Title (ownership certificate) transferred to your name
- Keys handed over (you can now occupy)

**Down payment:**
- Typically 20% of purchase price paid at completion
- Option fee (1%) credited; so you pay additional 19%
- Example: $585k price, 1% option fee already paid, additional 19% = $111,150 due at completion

**CPF withdrawal & refund:**
- Your CPF is used for part of down payment (if you nominated CPF payment)
- CPF board processes refund: Principal withdrawal + accrued interest repaid
- Calculation: Sale proceeds → CPF refund deducted → Mortgage discharged → Agent commission → Your net proceeds
- CPF refund often reduces your net cash receipt by 20–40% (accrued interest is substantial)

**Timing is critical:**
- Completion must occur within a specific date (usually 8–12 weeks from OTP)
- If either party delays, they are in breach
- Penalty: Interest accrual on delayed completion (typically 4–6% per annum)

**What you must have:**
- Final mortgage funds approved (bank transfers on completion day)
- CPF top-up approved (if using CPF)
- Insurance arranged (lender requires proof before releasing funds)
- Lawyer handling conveyancing (you do not go to bank/CPF yourself)

**Pitfall:** Assuming you will receive large cash cheque at completion. After CPF refund, agent commission, and costs, net cash is often 20–40% of price. Set realistic expectations.

#### Phase 7: Post-Completion

**What happens:**
- You are now the legal owner
- You can occupy property
- You are liable for property tax (from completion date onward)
- You are responsible for maintenance, insurance, utilities
- Seller is no longer liable for any defects

**Key point:** Unlike new launches, resale property has NO defects liability period. You accept property "as-is" at completion. If issues arise post-completion, seller is not responsible.

**Pitfall:** Discovering issues post-completion. You have no recourse. This is why pre-completion inspection is important.

### Financial Progression (Example: $585k property)

| Phase | Payment | Cumulative | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| OTP signing (option fee) | $5,850 (1%) | $5,850 | Non-refundable; deadline 5 days cooling-off |
| Completion (down payment) | $111,150 (19%) | $117,000 (20% total) | Option fee credited; you pay additional 19% |
| **Mortgage** | | | $468,000 (80% LTV; bank releases at completion) |
| **CPF refund deduction** | -$40,000–$60,000 | **Net cash to you $40,000–$60,000** | After CPF + agent commission (~3%) + legal costs (~$2,000) |

## Buying Resale HDB

### Timeline Overview
- Finding unit: 2–12 weeks (market dependent)
- Offer & negotiation: 1–4 weeks
- LOO (Letter of Offer) signing: 1 week
- Resale application: 1–2 weeks (concurrent with LOO)
- Completion: 8–12 weeks after LOO

### Step-by-Step Process

#### Phase 1: Unit Search & MOP Verification (Weeks 1–12)

**What happens:**
- Search HDB portal (official listing) or agent listings
- View units; shortlist based on location, size, price, condition
- Agent enquires if seller has completed 5-year MOP
  - MOP = Minimum Occupation Period
  - Seller must have owned for ≥5 years from completion date
  - If not completed, property CANNOT be sold (legal requirement)

**MOP verification:**
- Your agent checks with seller's agent or HDB directly
- Must verify seller's purchase date to confirm MOP is complete
- If MOP not complete, do not proceed (sale is void)

**What you are NOT committed to:**
- Viewing and enquiry are entirely optional; you can walk away anytime.

**Pitfall:** Assuming seller is eligible. Always verify MOP. I've seen deals collapse at final stages because seller purchased only 4 years ago and had not completed 5-year MOP.

#### Phase 2: Offer & Negotiation (Weeks 2–5)

**What happens:**
- Agent brings you and seller's agent together (usually don't meet directly)
- You make offer (typically 3–5% below asking)
- Seller counter-offers
- Negotiation until agreement on price

**Typical negotiation:**
- Asking price: $320,000
- Your offer: $310,000
- Seller counter: $318,000
- You counter: $315,000
- **Agreed: $316,000**

**What you are NOT committed to:**
- Verbal offer is not binding (same as private resale)
- You can walk away without penalty

**What you must do BEFORE making offer:**
- Get mortgage pre-approval from HDB or private bank
- Verify CPF account has sufficient funds for down payment
- Understand HDB valuation will impact CPF withdrawal

**Pitfall:** Not confirming HDB valuation upfront. HDB loans must be with HDB or HDB-approved bank. Valuation is HDB's assessment, not your purchase price. If purchase $316k but HDB values at $300k, CPF withdrawal capped at $300k; you pay $16k cash extra (not anticipated).

#### Phase 3: LOO (Letter of Offer) Signing (Week 3–4)

**What happens:**
- Seller makes formal offer (LOO) through agent
- LOO contains: Property address, sale price, completion date, special conditions
- You review LOO with lawyer
- You sign LOO to accept offer

**LOO is binding:**
Unlike private resale (where cooling-off applies), HDB resale LOO is BINDING once signed.

Once LOO is signed:
- You are legally committed to purchase
- Seller is legally committed to sell
- You cannot back out without legal consequences (lose deposit)

**Deposit:**
- Typically 1–2% of purchase price paid with LOO ($3,160–$6,320 on $316k)
- Refundable if deal falls through due to seller's fault (eg. seller withdraws)
- Forfeited if you withdraw

**What you must verify BEFORE signing LOO:**
1. **MOP completion date:** Verify seller purchased ≥5 years ago
2. **Seller's mortgage status:** Does seller still owe mortgage? (HDB checks this; must be discharged)
3. **Property condition:** Understand HDB will NOT fix post-sale defects
4. **Valuation:** Confirm expected HDB valuation (impacts CPF withdrawal)

**Pitfall:** Signing LOO without verifying MOP or HDB valuation. Both are critical; once LOO signed, you are bound even if valuation comes back lower than expected.

#### Phase 4: Resale Application & Mortgage Processing (Weeks 3–8)

**What happens (concurrent with LOO):**
- Your lawyer prepares resale application (form + documents) for HDB
- Application includes: Purchase price, buyer and seller info, property details, intended financing
- Submitted to HDB (Singapore's resale platform)
- HDB reviews and approves (typically 1–2 weeks)
- Meanwhile, your mortgage bank processes loan application:
  - Valuation survey (bank assesses property value)
  - Credit check
  - Income verification
  - TDSR/MSR calculation
  - Approval issued (conditional on completion and insurance)

**HDB valuation:**
- HDB conducts independent valuation
- Result: Market assessment of property value
- This becomes the ceiling for CPF withdrawal
- Example: Agreed price $316k, HDB valuation $310k → CPF withdrawable only up to $310k

**Mortgage approval:**
- Bank confirms loan amount (e.g., $253k if 80% LTV on $316k)
- Conditions: Insurance, CPF transfer, clear title
- Pre-approval letter issued

**What you must do:**
- Respond to HDB application promptly (provide missing documents)
- Respond to bank's mortgage conditions (insurance, CPF paperwork)
- Arrange insurance (mandatory; lender requires proof)

**Pitfall:** Delays in HDB application or mortgage processing. If HDB or bank slow, completion date may be pushed. Confirm timeline with agents/lawyers early.

#### Phase 5: Completion (Weeks 8–12 from LOO)

**What happens:**
- HDB approves resale; property is now in "registered state" (ownership transfer pending)
- Your lawyer coordinates CPF transfer and mortgage drawdown
- On completion date:
  - CPF is withdrawn from your account(s)
  - Mortgage funds are released by bank
  - Seller's outstanding mortgage is discharged
  - Title is transferred to your name (HDB updates registry)
  - Keys are handed over

**CPF transfer:**
- You must have initiated CPF application before completion
- CPF board processes transfer (typically 1–2 weeks)
- Funds go to your bank account, then to lawyer's client account (held in trust)
- At completion, CPF funds are used to pay HDB (via lawyer)

**Down payment:**
- Typically 20% of purchase price
- Deposit paid with LOO (1–2%) is credited
- Remaining 18–19% due at completion
- Example: $316k price, $4,740 deposit credited, additional $56,720 due at completion

**Financial flow:**
- Sale price: $316,000
- CPF withdrawal (up to valuation): $310,000 (capped by HDB valuation)
- CPF refund (principal + accrued interest): -$250,000 - $35,000 = -$285,000
- Down payment from CPF: $25,000 (difference between CPF withdrawal and refund owed)
- Down payment from cash: $31,000 (to make up 20% down payment)
- Mortgage: $252,800 (80% LTV)
- **Your net cash out: $31,000 (approx.)**

**Timing:**
- Completion typically 8–12 weeks after LOO
- Must be within contractual timeframe (breach if exceeded)
- Both parties coordinate to ensure all conditions met

**What you must have on completion day:**
- Funds ready (down payment + closing costs)
- Insurance in place
- CPF approved and ready for transfer
- Lawyer handling conveyancing

#### Phase 6: Post-Completion

**What happens:**
- You are now legal owner (HDB registry updated)
- You can collect keys and occupy property
- You are liable for property tax (from completion date onward)
- HDB maintenance charges begin (monthly)
- Any defects in property are your responsibility (seller not liable)

**Pitfall:** Same as private resale — no defects liability post-completion. Accept property "as-is".

### HDB Resale vs BTO Decision

| Factor | BTO | Resale |
|---|---|---|
| **Timeline** | 3–5 years wait for TOP | Immediate occupancy |
| **Price** | ~10–20% discount vs resale | Market price |
| **Condition** | New; no defects | Potentially older; defects possible |
| **Neighbourhood** | Developing; uncertainty | Established; known community |
| **MOP** | 5 years from TOP (so 8–10 years total wait) | 5 years from your purchase (simpler) |
| **Flexibility** | Must complete purchase post-TOP or lose booking | Can walk away before MOP expires (but at resale price loss) |
| **Risk appetite** | Low (patient, stable circumstances) | Moderate (need flexibility, inspectable) |

## Selling HDB

### Timeline Overview
- Listing: Weeks 1–2
- Offers & negotiation: Weeks 2–6
- LOO signing (by buyer): Week 3–4
- Resale application: Weeks 3–4
- Completion: Weeks 8–12 from LOO

### Step-by-Step Process

#### Phase 1: Preparation & Listing (Weeks 1–2)

**What you must do before listing:**
1. **Confirm MOP:** Have you owned for ≥5 years? If not, cannot sell.
2. **Outstanding mortgage:** Do you still owe HDB a mortgage? Must be disclosed; it will be discharged at completion.
3. **Property condition:** Minor repairs recommended (fresh paint, fix broken fittings). Clean property = faster sale.
4. **Pricing strategy:** Research recent sold prices in your neighbourhood (via URA caveats, agent estimates). Price competitively (not too high = no offers; not too low = leaving money on table).

**Listing:**
- Engage HDB-approved agent (or sell privately; private sales slower, require more effort)
- Agent takes photos, uploads to HDB portal, distributes to buyer agents
- Offers typically start coming within 1–2 weeks

**Pricing:**
- Typical HDB 4-room: $320,000–$420,000 (location dependent)
- Mature estates (30+ years): Lower price ($280,000–$350,000)
- Prime locations (near MRT, good schools): Higher ($380,000–$450,000)
- Condition: Newer units (recent renovation) command 5–10% premium

**Pitfall:** Overpricing. If you list at $420k but market value is $400k, you get zero offers. Wait 2 months, then reduce price (wasted time, looks desperate). Better to price correctly upfront.

#### Phase 2: Offers & Negotiation (Weeks 2–6)

**What happens:**
- Buyer agents approach; make offers
- You negotiate (through your agent) to reach agreement on price
- Seller (you) makes counter-offer; buyer accepts or counters

**Typical flow:**
- Buyer offer: $360,000 (aiming low)
- Your counter: $375,000
- Buyer counter: $368,000
- **Agreed: $370,000**

**Negotiation tactics:**
- Start with asking price 2–3% above target (leaves room to negotiate down)
- Hold firm if offers are unrealistic (don't desperately accept lowball)
- Speed matters: If market slow, must be more flexible. If market hot, can hold firmer.

**What you can negotiate beyond price:**
- Completion timeline (buyer wants fast; you may want slower)
- Seller contributions (buyer asks seller to fix issues; negotiate cost)
- Chattels (included items: air con, fridge, etc.; worth negotiating)

**Pitfall:** Accepting first offer without negotiating. Buyers often start low; counter is standard.

#### Phase 3: Buyer's LOO & Your Agreement (Week 3–4)

**What happens:**
- Agreement reached on price ($370,000 in example)
- Buyer signs LOO (makes formal offer to you)
- Your agent brings LOO to you
- You decide: Accept (sign), counter, or reject

**If you accept LOO:**
- You are now bound to sell (cannot back out without penalty)
- Buyer is bound to buy
- Both parties sign LOO; legally binding contract formed

**Deposit (from buyer's perspective):**
- Buyer pays 1–2% deposit with LOO
- This is held by agent (in trust account) until completion
- If buyer backs out, you get to keep deposit
- If you back out, buyer gets refund + damages claim

**What you must verify in LOO:**
- Price (obviously)
- Completion date (must be realistic; allows time for buyer's mortgage processing)
- Special conditions (e.g., "subject to buyer obtaining HDB loan approval")

**Pitfall:** Accepting unrealistic completion date. Buyer needs 8–12 weeks to process mortgage and HDB application. If you agree to 6-week completion, buyer may breach. Realistic timeline = better chance of smooth completion.

#### Phase 4: Discharge Mortgage & Prepare Documents (Weeks 3–8)

**What you must do:**
1. **Outstanding mortgage:** Notify HDB of pending sale. HDB will discharge mortgage from sale proceeds.
2. **Defect repairs (optional):** Some buyers ask seller to fix issues. You can negotiate cost or refuse (property is as-is).
3. **Documents:** Prepare LOO, property documents, ownership proof for buyer's lawyer.

**Discharge process:**
- HDB holds mortgage on property
- At completion, buyer's funds are directed to pay HDB first (clears mortgage)
- Remaining funds go to you (after agent commission)

**Your net proceeds:**
- Sale price: $370,000
- HDB mortgage discharge: Approximately $200,000–$250,000 (if you still owe)
- Agent commission: 1–1.5% = $5,550–$5,775
- CPF refund (from your original purchase): -$200,000 - $30,000 accrued = -$230,000
- **Your net proceeds: $370,000 - $230,000 - $5,550 = $134,450** (approx.)

Note: If you still have outstanding HDB mortgage, the amount discharged reduces your proceeds further.

**Pitfall:** Assuming you will receive $370k. CPF refund is large; after that and commission, net is often 30–40% of price.

#### Phase 5: Completion (Weeks 8–12 from LOO)

**What happens:**
- Buyer's lawyer coordinates with HDB and your lawyer
- On completion date:
  - Buyer's down payment + mortgage funds paid to your lawyer
  - HDB mortgage discharged
  - Title transferred to buyer
  - Keys handed over
  - You receive net proceeds (after all deductions)

**Your role:**
- Be available to hand over keys
- Sign completion documents
- Vacate property (coordinate moving date)

**Timing:**
- Completion must occur within contractual date (usually 8–12 weeks)
- If either party delays, they pay interest on outstanding amount

**Pitfall:** Not vacating by completion date. If you are still in property on completion, buyer has right to sue for damages. Coordinate moving date well in advance.

#### Phase 6: Post-Completion

**What you must do:**
- Update address with HDB (moving notification)
- Update address with other agencies (IRAS, utilities, etc.)
- CPF refund processing (HDB processes and credits CPF account, then releases cash balance to you)

**CPF refund mechanics:**
- Proceeds held in your CPF account initially
- Principal withdrawn + accrued interest repaid to CPF
- Remaining cash released to your bank account (within 1–2 weeks post-completion)

## Selling Private Property

### Timeline Overview
- Listing: Weeks 1–2
- Offers & negotiation: Weeks 2–8 (market dependent)
- OTP signing (by buyer): Week 3–4
- OTP exercise: 3 weeks from OTP signing
- Completion: 8–12 weeks from OTP exercise

### Selling Process (Abbreviated vs HDB)

**Key differences from HDB:**
1. No HDB approval required (private transactions are bilateral)
2. Buyer's agent brings offer; you negotiate directly with buyer (via agents)
3. OTP is binding contract (same as buying resale private, but reversed)
4. Completion 8–12 weeks later
5. No CPF refund mechanics (those apply only to HDB/EC property)

### Step-by-Step (Abbreviated)

#### Phase 1: Listing
- Engage agent (same as HDB)
- Price strategically (compare to recent sales in building/area)
- Market aggressively (best agents bring multiple offers quickly)

#### Phase 2: Negotiation
- Buyers (via agents) make offers
- You negotiate to reach agreement
- Once agreed, buyer signs OTP (formal offer)

#### Phase 3: Buyer's OTP & 5-Day Cooling-Off
- Buyer has 5-day inspection/cooling-off period
- Buyer can back out within 5 days (loses option fee, typically 1%)
- After 5 days, buyer is bound

#### Phase 4: Completion (8–12 weeks)
- Both parties coordinate with lawyers
- Seller's outstanding mortgage (if any) discharged from sale proceeds
- Title transferred to buyer
- Keys handed over
- Proceeds paid to you (less agent commission ~1–1.5%, lawyer costs ~$2,000)

#### Phase 5: Post-Completion
- Update address with relevant agencies
- Capital gains tax: Singapore does NOT tax capital gains on property. You keep 100% of proceeds (minus agent/legal costs).
- SSD (if applicable): If you held property <3 years, SSD is due. This is seller's responsibility; deducted from proceeds.

### SSD Impact on Seller

**If held 1–2 years:**
- Gain from sale might be $50,000
- SSD 12% (within 1 year) or 8% (1–2 years)
- Example: Sell at $650k, bought at $600k, SSD 8% = $52,000 (on $650k sale price)
- SSD eats much of the gain

**If held 3+ years:**
- SSD = 0%
- Keep 100% of gain

**Pitfall:** Flipping (buying and selling <3 years) is expensive. SSD kills profitability. Most investors hold 3+ years.

### Coordination: Selling & Buying Simultaneously

Many upgraders must coordinate selling their old property and buying a new one at the same time. This is complex and risky.

## Simultaneous Buy-Sell Coordination: The Critical Timeline Alignment

### The Core Problem

You are selling HDB for $370,000 and buying private for $600,000. Timeline:
- HDB sale completion: Scheduled Week 12
- Private purchase completion: Scheduled Week 10 (2 weeks BEFORE HDB completion)

**This timing mismatch is lethal.**

You must pay private property down payment at Week 10 ($150,000+), but you only receive HDB sale proceeds at Week 12. You are short $150,000 for 2 weeks. Options:
1. **Bridge loan:** Borrow $150k for 2 weeks at 6–8% interest (expensive; ~$200 cost)
2. **Delay private completion:** Renegotiate to Week 13 or later (buyer may refuse if they have their own timeline)
3. **Advance HDB completion:** Try to fast-track HDB sale to Week 10 (challenging; lenders/HDB may not allow)
4. **Additional cash buffer:** Have $150k sitting in savings (inefficient use of capital)

### Optimal Sequencing

**Best practice: Sell BEFORE you buy.**

1. **Sell HDB:** Complete at Week 8 → Receive proceeds Week 9
2. **Confirm proceeds received:** Week 9–10
3. **BUY private:** Sign OTP Week 11 → Complete Week 19 (8 weeks later)

This sequence avoids the double-holding period (owning both properties simultaneously).

**Advantages:**
- No bridge loan needed
- Simpler ABSD calculation (no brief period of owning 2 properties)
- Financially safer (proceeds in hand before new purchase)

**Disadvantages:**
- Risk: Private property you want might be sold to someone else while you are selling HDB
- Temporary homelessness: Gap between HDB completion and private occupancy (weeks/months)

### Managing the Gap: Temporary Housing

If you must have a gap (e.g., private only completes after HDB completion):
1. **Rent short-term:** Rent a flat for 2–4 weeks ($2,000–$4,000/week) while waiting for keys
2. **Stay with family:** Impose on relatives temporarily
3. **Hotel:** Expensive but option if short gap (<1 week)

Most upgraders accept 2–4 week gap and budget for short-term rental.

### Scenario: Multiple Offers Strategy

To avoid the timing squeeze, experienced upgraders:
1. Start selling HDB early (list 3 months before planned upgrade)
2. Parallel search for private property
3. Once HDB sale agreement reached and completion date locked, start serious private property bidding
4. Time OTP for private to occur 2–3 weeks after HDB completion date
5. This gives ~12 weeks for private completion, by which time you have HDB proceeds

This requires planning 6+ months ahead but significantly reduces financing risk.

### ABSD Implications of Double-Holding

**Scenario: Own both HDB + private simultaneously (even briefly)**

Singapore citizen, 2nd property rule:
- HDB ownership does NOT count towards ABSD (citizens exempt)
- But private property ownership is still 2nd property
- If you own HDB + private simultaneously, private is "2nd property" → ABSD 7%
- If HDB sold first, then private bought 1 week later, private is "1st property" → ABSD 0%

**Difference:** $42,000 in ABSD on $600k property (7% vs 0%).

**Solution:** Ensure HDB sale COMPLETES and funds released BEFORE you EXERCISE OTP on private. Exercise, not signing OTP is the moment you become legally committed; funds in hand by then = 1st property status.

## Key Timelines & Red Flags

### New Launch Private
- **Expected timeline:** 16–20 weeks (booking → OTP exercise → completion)
- **Red flag:** Developer can't commit to TOP date; construction delays endemic
- **What to do:** Build in 6-month buffer; don't assume TOP happens on schedule

### Resale Private
- **Expected timeline:** 12–14 weeks (offer → OTP → completion)
- **Red flag:** Buyer's mortgage slow, or HDB/CPF delays
- **What to do:** Chase agents/banks weekly; confirm timeline with both sides

### Resale HDB
- **Expected timeline:** 12–14 weeks (offer → LOO → HDB resale application → completion)
- **Red flag:** HDB application slow, or buyer's loan approval delayed
- **What to do:** Same as private; push for updates

### Critical Binding Points (Do NOT Confuse These)

| Transaction Type | Binding Point | When You Can Still Walk Away | Penalty |
|---|---|---|---|
| **New launch private** | OTP exercise | Before OTP signing | Loss of booking fee (~2%) |
| | | Within 3 weeks of OTP | Loss of option fee (~5%) |
| | | After 3 weeks OTP | Full purchase liability (breach of contract) |
| **Resale private** | 5-day cooling-off expires | Within 5 days of OTP signing | Loss of option fee (~1%) |
| | | After 5 days | Full purchase liability |
| **Resale HDB** | LOO signing | Before signing | None (no commitment yet) |
| | | After signing | Forfeit deposit (~1–2%); possible damages |

## Universal Risk Mitigation

Regardless of transaction type, mitigate these risks:

1. **Always get mortgage pre-approval before making offer.** If pre-approval falls through post-binding commitment, you are liable for breach.

2. **Verify property title & MOP status upfront.** Don't assume seller is eligible to sell.

3. **Coordinate timing with agent/lawyer.** Understand exact completion dates; don't assume "8 weeks" if it could be 12.

4. **Arrange insurance early.** Lenders won't release funds without proof; delays otherwise.

5. **Model CPF refund conservatively.** Expect accrued interest to be larger than you think.

6. **Don't overestimate sale proceeds.** Budget for agent commission, legal costs, CPF refund, and any outstanding mortgage.

7. **Stress-test affordability on single income.** If couple, model TDSR on lower income alone (in case of job loss).

8. **Build 3–6 month buffer into timeline.** Delays happen; don't plan exactly to completion date.

## Exit Timing Decision Framework

The most expensive mistake a buyer makes is **choosing the wrong exit window**. A 3-year hold can cost $50–100K more in Seller's Stamp Duty (SSD) than a 5-year hold on the same property. Yet most buyers don't think about exit strategy until they *need* to exit. This section provides a framework for deciding hold period at purchase, not at exit.

### The SSD Cliff: The 36-Month Threshold

Seller's Stamp Duty drops dramatically at 36 months:

| Hold Period | SSD Rate | Example: $1.65M Sale | SSD Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Month 24 (2yr) | 8% | $1,650,000 | $132,000 |
| Month 35 (3yr) | 4% | $1,650,000 | $66,000 |
| **Month 36 (3yr)** | **0%** | **$1,650,000** | **$0** |
| Month 60 (5yr) | 0% | $1,650,000 | $0 |

**The swing**: Between Month 35 and Month 36, SSD *disappears*. This is a binary cliff, not a gradient. On a $1.65M property, it's a $66,000 swing — equivalent to **40+ months of rental income** on a $1,500/mo tenant.

### Hold Period Sweep: Finding Your Breakeven

Before purchase, use the **Hold Period Sweep** tool in the Deep Dive tab to see what IRR you'll achieve at different hold periods under bear/base/bull scenarios.

**Example table** (fictional property, $800K purchase, 70% LTV, 3% rate, $1,500/mo carry):

| Hold (months) | Exit Price (Base) | Net Cash | SSD Active | IRR | Hit 10% Target? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 12 | $850K | $12K | Yes (12%) | 1.5% | ✗ |
| 24 | $920K | $56K | Yes (8%) | 6.2% | ✗ |
| 30 | $960K | $78K | Yes (4%) | 8.8% | ✗ |
| **36** | **$1,000K** | **$142K** | **No** | **11.5%** | **✓** |
| 48 | $1,080K | $195K | No | 13.2% | ✓ |
| 60 | $1,160K | $250K | No | 14.5% | ✓ |

Notice:
- Rows 12–30 don't hit the 10% target IRR.
- Month 36 row has bold green highlight: SSD expires, IRR jumps, and target is met.
- Every additional month after Month 36 improves IRR slightly due to time-value math, but the big cliff is at Month 36.

**What to discuss with client at purchase**:
- "Based on your target IRR, you need to hold for at least 36–48 months for this to work."
- If client says "I might need to exit in 2 years," either (a) find a different property with better cash flow, or (b) reset expectations to 6–8% IRR.
- Document the intended hold period in the client file. Revisit it annually.

### Forced Exit Scenarios: When the Plan Breaks

Life happens. Job transfers, divorces, market crashes, medical emergencies. A good exit strategy accounts for forced exits.

#### Scenario 1: Job Transfer (Month 24, forced exit)

**Client situation**: $800K property, bought 2 years ago, but job transferred to Sydney. Must sell within 60 days.

**Problem**: SSD is still active (8% on base-case exit price).

**Solutions**:
1. **Rent it out** for another 12 months via agent; fly back for viewings quarterly. Requires rental income to exceed carry cost + management fees (typically 8% of rent). 
   - Pro: Hit the SSD cliff, avoid $66K penalty.
   - Con: Landlord liability, tenant risk, currency exposure if income is in AUD.
   
2. **Exit now and absorb SSD** if the sale price is high enough to overcome it.
   - Calculate: Net proceeds at Month 24 with 8% SSD vs projected proceeds if rented another 12 months.
   - If bear-case exit price at Month 36 *minus* rental carry costs exceeds current-market net proceeds, rent it out.
   
3. **Sell on a loss** (if bear-case prevails). Cut losses early rather than hoping.
   - Use the Required Exit Price Solver to see what exit price you need to break even; compare to current market listings.

#### Scenario 2: Personal Circumstance (Job loss, health, family)

Forced exits driven by job loss or medical emergency usually can't wait 36 months.

**Mitigation at purchase**:
- Ensure the property has positive cash flow (rent > carry) so it can be sustained as a rental for 2–3 years while searching for new income.
- Keep 6–12 months liquid buffer in emergency fund (non-negotiable). Never borrow the full acquisition cost.
- Stress-test affordability: Can the client sustain carry costs on *spouse's income alone*? If married.

**At exit time**:
- Weigh net proceeds *today* vs projected proceeds in 12 months.
- If both spouses working, ensure one has stable income to cover carry costs during hold-over.

#### Scenario 3: Market Crash (Month 24, bear case realized)

Property has depreciated 10%. Exit price is now $720K instead of $920K (base case).

**Decision logic**:
1. Calculate net proceeds under bear case *with* SSD (Month 24): $720K − 8% SSD − loan − legal ≈ $480K net.
2. Project bear-case exit at Month 60 (no SSD): $720K × 1.02^3 (2% CAGR) ≈ $765K; net ≈ $590K.
3. Is the extra $110K worth 36 more months of negative cash flow? Depends on monthly carry cost.
   - If carry is $1,500/mo and property has rent of $1,800/mo, you're *positive* $300/mo. Hold it.
   - If carry is $2,000/mo with no rent (owner-occupied), you're negative $2,000/mo. Cut losses at Month 24 and reset capital elsewhere.

**Key insight**: A forced exit in a bear market is *not* a reason to panic-sell at the worst moment. If the property cash-flows positively, rent it out and wait for the cycle. If it cash-flows negatively and you have no emergency buffer, exit and redeploy capital to higher-conviction plays.

### Stress-Testing Affordability: Single-Income Scenario

When advising a client, always ask: "If one of you lost your job tomorrow, could you sustain this property for 12 months without selling?"

**Worked example**:
- Couple: HHI $180K ($90K each)
- Property: $800K, 70% LTV, carry cost $1,500/mo
- Rental income: $1,800/mo (covers carry with $300/mo buffer)

**Stress test**: Spouse A loses job.
- Remaining income: $90K/yr = $7,500/mo gross
- Affordability on remaining property + carry: TDSR check at $7,500 income
  - If they hit TDSR ceiling (55%), they can still afford carry; might need to cut discretionary spending.
  - If rental income is passive (not affected by job loss), property sustains itself.
  
**Verdict**: Safe to hold property even on single income, because rent covers carry.

**Alternative scenario**: Client is self-employed (volatile income).
- Build in 6 months of carry costs as emergency buffer *at purchase*.
- Property must be cash-flow positive or near-breakeven.
- Never buy a property that *requires* your full income to sustain carry.

### Decision Framework: When to Hold vs When to Exit

| Signal | Hold (Wait for Month 36+) | Exit Now (Absorb SSD) |
|---|---|---|
| **Cash flow** | Positive (rent > carry) | Negative or breakeven |
| **Market** | Stable or appreciating | Crashing; capital better deployed elsewhere |
| **Personal** | Stable; no life event | Job loss, relocation, medical emergency |
| **Target IRR** | Month 36 clears it | Month 36 still below target |
| **Carry burden** | Manageable on single income | Unsustainable; client stressed |

### Strategic Principle: Plan the Exit at Purchase

**The best time to decide your exit strategy is at purchase, not at exit.** 

When a client is signing contracts, ask:
- "When do you see yourself exiting this property? 3 years, 5 years, 10 years?"
- "What's your target annual return? 6%, 10%, 15%?"
- "If you had to exit in an emergency (job loss, medical), could you sustain carry costs on your spouse's income alone?"
- "Are you buying for capital appreciation, cash flow, or both?"

Use the **Hold Period Sweep** and **Required Exit Price Solver** tools to show the client exactly what IRR they'll achieve at their intended hold period under base/bear/bull scenarios. Write it down. File it. Revisit annually.

When an exit *actually* becomes necessary — whether planned or forced — the decision logic is already clear. You're not making emotional decisions in a pressure situation; you're executing a strategy laid out 3–5 years ago.
