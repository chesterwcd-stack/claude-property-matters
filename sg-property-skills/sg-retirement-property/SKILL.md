# Singapore Property Retirement Planning

Property is often the largest asset in a Singaporean household's portfolio. This skill treats property not as a standalone purchase but as part of a lifetime financial strategy — with retirement as the ultimate horizon. The goal is to ensure every property decision moves you closer to retirement readiness, not further away.

## Core Frameworks

**1. Property Lifecycle Planning** → `references/retirement-timeline.md`

Map major property milestones against life stages: first property (25-35), first upgrade (32-42), peak earning years (40-55), pre-retirement window (50-62), and retirement transition (62+). Align holding periods with SSD timing, MOP eligibility, CPF withdrawal ages (55, 65), and statutory retirement age progression (62/63 rising to 65 by 2030). Don't plan exit strategies in isolation—nest them in a 40-year property journey.

**2. Asset Growth & Portfolio Strategy** → `references/asset-growth.md`

Model property appreciation using historical benchmarks (3-5% p.a. nominal, 1-3% real after inflation) and location-specific catalysts (URA Master Plan, MRT expansion, supply constraints). Calculate total return by accounting for all holding costs (BSD, ABSD, SSD, maintenance, mortgage interest, CPF refund). Evaluate three core strategies: sell 1 buy 1 better (trade for stronger growth), sell 1 buy 2 (split into residence + investment), consolidate 2 into 1 (simplify, de-risk approaching retirement).

**3. Exit Strategies** → `references/exit-strategies.md`

Build optionality by stress-testing six exit paths: downsize (most common), sell and rent (maximum cash release), right-size (different property, same net effect), monetise via rental (ongoing income), en-bloc windfall (unpredictable but high-reward), and lease buyback (HDB specific). Evaluate lease decay risk—a 65-year-old holding a property with 40 years remaining lease faces financing constraints and falling resale value. Always map exit timing against SSD expiry and market cycle.

**4. Investment Diversification** → `references/portfolio-balance.md`

Most Singapore households have 60-80% of net worth in property—this is concentration risk. Build a balanced portfolio by life stage: early career (50-70% property, 5-15% investments), mid-career (40-60% property, 15-25% investments), pre-retirement (30-50% property, 15-25% investments), retirement (20-40% property, income-generating assets). Use CPF (especially SA compounding at 4%), ETFs, REITs, and SSB to reduce property concentration without sacrificing growth.

## Key Principles

- **Every property decision should be evaluated against the retirement horizon.** A large mortgage at age 55 is a different bet than at 35.
- **Property concentration is real risk.** You cannot sell 10% of your condo. You cannot diversify a single property. Plan accordingly.
- **Lease decay is a retirement landmine.** Don't hold a 60-year-old property with 40 years remaining lease into retirement. Financing and resale value collapse below remaining lease of ~40 years.
- **Challenge the "property always goes up" assumption.** Singapore's government actively manages supply, cooling measures, and policy—this reduces volatility but also caps upside and creates policy risk.
- **Optionality is the retirement goal.** Enough liquid assets to choose your lifestyle (downsize, rent, move closer to family, relocate overseas) in your 60s. Don't sacrifice it for over-leveraged property.
- **The property ladder is not one-way.** You don't have to upgrade infinitely. Right-sizing and consolidating are legitimate wealth strategies.

## When to Use This Skill

- Planning first property purchase or upgrade (and how it fits retirement)
- Modelling long-term appreciation and exit timing
- Deciding whether to hold, sell, or invest in additional property
- Assessing property as a portfolio concentration risk
- Planning downsizing or right-sizing in pre-retirement years
- Managing SSD timing, ABSD impact, or CPF withdrawal strategy
- Comparing property returns to alternative investments (CPF, equities, REITs)
- Stress-testing retirement readiness against property value swings
