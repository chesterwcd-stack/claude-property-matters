# Exit Strategies: How to Cash Out in Retirement

Owning property is not the same as having a retirement plan. This guide covers six concrete exit paths and the decision framework for choosing the right one.

## Six Exit Options Explained

### Exit Option 1: Sell and Downsize (Most Common)

**What it is:**
Sell your larger primary residence, buy a smaller property, pocket the difference as retirement cash.

**Mechanics:**
- Current property: $2.5M, fully paid off (age 65)
- Sell for: $2.5M
- Costs (agent 1.25%, legal, capital gains if applicable): -$70K
- Net proceeds: $2.43M
- New property: $1.2M (1BR or 2BR compact condo, HDB, or downsized EC)
- Costs (BSD 1.5%, legal, renovation): -$50K
- Total outlay for new property: $1.25M
- **Cash released: $2.43M – $1.25M = $1.18M**

**Lifestyle change:**
- Move from 4BR spacious condo to 1-2BR compact unit
- Reduce maintenance burden (smaller unit, fewer repairs, easier cleaning)
- Proximity to family or amenities (choose location for retirement lifestyle, not resale value)
- Loss of "spare rooms" (no guest bedroom, office space, or hobby room)

**When it works:**
- You're 62–68 years old (young enough to enjoy new place, establish routines)
- Downsize is not traumatic (kids grown, single or couple only)
- You need liquidity (pension is modest, investment returns are lower, or plan overseas travel)
- New property has good remaining lease (>50 years minimum)

**Why it's most common:**
- Psychologically clear: sell one, buy one smaller, pocket difference
- Financially straightforward: one exit, one entry, done
- Housing needs often align: fewer rooms needed in retirement
- Creates tangible cash buffer for unexpected expenses

**Risks:**
- Emotional attachment: your family home has memories; moving can be difficult
- Downsizing overestimation: you think you need 2BR but realize you need 3BR once settled (then regret sale)
- Transaction costs are high: selling $2.5M + buying $1.2M = ~$120K in combined costs, eating into gains
- Timing risk: if property market crashes post-60, you're downizing into a worse market
- New property lease decay: ensure new property has 50+ years remaining lease

**Retirement impact (positive):**
- $1.18M in liquid reserves = 20–30 years of expenses (if expenses are $40–60K/year)
- Primary residence is smaller, paid off, low ongoing costs
- Better preservation of capital: you have optionality if health crisis or family emergency arises
- Peace of mind: "downsizing done, retirement cash in hand"

**Retirement impact (risks):**
- No large asset to leave as legacy (unless you invest the $1.18M aggressively, which adds risk)
- If you need to move again (e.g., health care proximity), you've already downsized once; further downsizing is constrained
- New market conditions: $1.2M buys a different (smaller) property in future; what if property inflation is high?

**Execution timeline:**
- Age 55–60: identify target downsize property, monitor market
- Age 60–62: list current property for sale, secure buyers
- Age 62–65: complete sale, purchase new property, settle in
- Target: fully settled and liquidity in place before age 65–67

### Exit Option 2: Sell and Rent (Maximum Cash Release)

**What it is:**
Sell entire property, pocket full proceeds, rent housing. Invest proceeds for ongoing income.

**Mechanics:**
- Current property: $2.5M, fully paid off (age 65)
- Sell for: $2.5M; costs: -$70K; net proceeds: $2.43M
- Rent housing: 3BR in Singapore = $3.5K–$4.5K/month, assume $4K = $48K/year
- Invest proceeds: $2.43M at 4% yield = $97K/year (conservative estimate)
- **Net retirement income from property: $97K/year (investment) – $48K/year (rent) = $49K/year net**

**Lifestyle change:**
- No home ownership; renting in retirement (psychological shift for many)
- Maximum flexibility: can relocate within Singapore (upscale rental, downscale rental, or move to HDB area)
- No maintenance responsibility: landlord handles repairs, maintenance, utilities
- Psychological burden: "throwing rent away" mentality (though economically, you're earning investment returns to cover rent)

**When it works:**
- Your property's imputed rental yield is very low (<2%): i.e., the rent you'd pay to occupy it is much less than 2% of the property's value
- Example: $2.5M property rents for $4K/month = 1.9% gross yield. Investing $2.5M at 4% gives $100K/year. Rent cost is $48K/year. Net gain: $52K/year from the arbitrage.
- You want maximum flexibility (ability to relocate, explore living overseas, or move closer to family without property friction)
- You have health reasons for not wanting to manage property (repairs, maintenance, property agent coordination)
- You prefer simplicity: one asset class (investments) rather than juggling property + investments

**Advantages:**
- Maximum cash release: $2.43M in full control, vs $1.18M in downsize scenario
- Flexibility to relocate: can rent in different area, with family, or even overseas
- No property surprise costs: landlord responsible for major repairs
- Investment portfolio is diversified: not concentrated in one illiquid asset
- Downside protection: if property market crashes, you're not exposed; if rental market crashes, you can move to cheaper rental

**Risks:**
- Rent inflation: if rent rises to $5K/month by age 70, your net income drops (investment returns may lag inflation)
- Investment returns uncertainty: assuming 4% is conservative, but bond returns may be lower, equity returns volatile
- Psychological aversion: many Singaporeans view renting as "losing" compared to owning
- Lack of legacy: no property to leave children (though you have liquid assets, which is also legacy)
- Inflation risk on rent: Singapore rent has historically grown 2–3% p.a.; over 20 years, $4K becomes $6K–$6.5K
- Rental market risk: during economic downturns, rental supply increases but demand decreases; you might find it hard to secure good rental at good price

**Retirement impact:**
- Income: $97K/year investment income – $48K/year rent = ~$49K/year net
- Flexibility: maximum optionality; can relocate, downsize rental, or relocate overseas
- Capital preservation: $2.43M invested at 4% is stable; no concentration risk
- Suitable if: you have CPF LIFE + investment income covering most expenses, and want maximum flexibility

**Execution timeline:**
- Age 55–60: start investing aggressively to build liquid assets parallel to property holding
- Age 60–62: decide on rental location (upscale, near family, good transport)
- Age 62: list property for sale
- Age 63–65: complete sale, secure rental lease, move, invest proceeds
- First few years in retirement: monitor rental costs, investment returns; adjust withdrawals

### Exit Option 3: Right-Size (Different Property, Same Net Effect)

**What it is:**
Move to a different property that better fits current needs. Not necessarily smaller, but more suitable for retirement lifestyle.

**Examples:**
- Move from walk-up (no lift) 4BR to lift-access 2BR (accessibility for ageing)
- Move from house (high maintenance garden, repairs) to condo with managed facilities
- Move from north Singapore to central location (closer to family, medical facilities, transport)
- Move from isolated condo to mature HDB estate with active community

**Mechanics:**
- Current property: $2.2M (walk-up, suburban)
- Sell for: $2.2M; costs: -$60K; net proceeds: $2.14M
- New property: $1.8M (lift-access, central, modern facilities)
- Costs: -$50K
- Net change: $2.14M – $1.8M = $340K cash released (or keep in reserve for contingencies)

**Lifestyle change:**
- Often improves retirement quality of life: better location, easier maintenance, age-friendly design
- Preserve "home ownership" psychology: you still own, just in a different property
- Redefine home: from accumulation (big, for family) to enjoyment (optimised for current phase)

**When it works:**
- Current property is not optimally suited to retirement (isolated, high maintenance, far from family)
- You want to move but don't need to drastically downsize (hence some capital release, but not "sell-and-rent" scale)
- Right-sizing captures gains from previous appreciation, redeployed into better-located asset
- Remaining life expectancy is long (you'll enjoy the new property for 20+ years)

**Advantages:**
- Improves retirement lifestyle without radical change (still own, just different property)
- Modest cash release: $340K in this example
- Location arbitrage: move to better-located property that may appreciate faster post-retirement (e.g., move to central as more wealthy retirees cluster there)
- Reduced ongoing costs: modern condo is more efficient than old walk-up; facilities are managed

**Risks:**
- Still concentrated in property: you're not diversifying away from real estate
- Emotional: leaving a long-held home is difficult for some (though less severe than renting)
- New property market risk: if you buy at peak, subsequent market decline affects new property
- Lease decay on new property: ensure new property has 50+ years remaining lease

**Retirement impact:**
- Housing cost: similar to downsize (fully paid, but maintenance may differ)
- Liquidity: $340K cash released (less than downsize, more than stay-put)
- Flexibility: moderate (still a property owner, so less flexible than renting, but more relocated than staying)
- Quality of life: potentially improved (better location, better design, lower maintenance)

### Exit Option 4: Monetise via Rental (Passive Income, No Exit)

**What it is:**
Keep property, rent it out in full, live elsewhere. Create ongoing income stream without selling.

**Mechanics:**
- Current property: $2.5M, fully paid off (age 62)
- Rent it out: $4K/month = $48K/year gross
- Maintenance & management: -$8K/year (repairs, property agent fee, insurance)
- Gross income: $40K/year
- Rental property tax (income tax at ~10–12% on rental income): -$4.8K
- **Net annual income: ~$35K/year** (ongoing, no exit, indefinite)

**Live elsewhere:**
- Smaller rental: $1.5K/month = $18K/year
- Or: with family (cost-free)
- Or: overseas (e.g., Malaysia, where rent is cheaper)

**Lifestyle change:**
- You maintain property ownership (psychological ownership, legacy for children)
- No primary residence "lock-in": you can relocate freely without selling
- Landlord responsibilities: tenant management, maintenance oversight, property agent coordination
- Income generator: $35K/year is meaningful in retirement

**When it works:**
- Property is fully paid off (mortgage eliminated; cash flow is mostly profit)
- Good rental location: near MRT, near employment hub, near schools, or in expat-friendly area
- You have alternative housing: don't need the capital to downsize
- You're disciplined as a landlord: able to manage tenants, negotiate leases, coordinate repairs
- Rental yield is healthy: 2% net yield ($50K net on $2.5M property)

**Advantages:**
- Passive income: $35K/year with no active work (after tenant is settled)
- Capital preservation: property is still yours; can be left to children
- Inflation hedge: rental income typically grows 2–3% p.a., outpacing inflation
- Flexibility: can relocate or downsize living arrangement without selling property
- Tax deductibility: maintenance, repairs, agent fees are deductible, reducing taxable rental income

**Risks:**
- Tenant risk: bad tenants, non-payment, damage, lengthy evictions (can lose 3–6 months income)
- Maintenance risk: surprise major expense (aircon, water damage, structural) costs $5K–$30K
- Vacancy risk: between tenants, property is unoccupied (0 income for 1–2 months)
- Rental market risk: recession reduces tenant demand, rental prices fall below $4K
- Complexity: landlord is not a passive role; requires active management, especially in disputes
- Regulatory risk: government could introduce rent control or tenant protections that reduce landlord returns
- Ageing landlord risk: at age 75–80, are you still able to manage a rental property? Exit plan needed

**Retirement impact:**
- Income: $35K/year ongoing
- Capital: $2.5M locked in property (cannot access without selling or mortgaging)
- Optionality: moderate (can relocate, but property is still a commitment)
- Dependency on property market health: if property value crashes, you don't lose it (still live elsewhere), but collateral value for emergency loan drops

**When to exit:**
- Age 75+: consider selling and downsize to simplify life
- Tenant problems escalate: protracted disputes, legal costs exceed rental income
- Your health declines: no longer able to manage property matters
- Better opportunity: rental market crashes but property value is still high; sell and invest elsewhere

**Execution timeline:**
- Age 55–62: improve property for rental appeal (renovation, painting, repairs)
- Age 62: list for rental, vet tenants carefully, secure 3-year tenancy if possible
- Age 62–65: settle into rental income, monitor tenant, coordinate maintenance
- Age 75+: consider exit strategy (downsize, sell, or hand to adult children)

### Exit Option 5: En-Bloc Sale (High-Reward, Unplanned)

**What it is:**
Collective sale of entire development to developer. Typically offers 20–30% premium over individual market value.

**Example:**
- Your property: $2.5M in individual market
- En-bloc sale price offered by developer: $3.2M
- Gross gain: $700K extra vs individual sale
- After costs (legal, agent, tax): net gain ~$600K

**Mechanics:**
- Requires 80% of unit owners to agree (collective sale agreement)
- Developer makes offer, legal process, completion (typically 1–3 years)
- You receive proceeds at completion

**When it happens:**
- High plot ratio gap: building is old, low density; developer can redevelop at much higher density (profit motive for developer)
- Large land plot: valuable in prime location; developer can assemble multiple units into larger plot
- Ageing building: 30–50 years old; redevelopment seen as superior to repair/maintain
- Favourable zoning: area is rezoned upmarket; land value has increased significantly
- Cannot be planned: depends on 80% agreement, developer interest, market conditions

**Probability factors:**
- Building age: 30+ years increases en-bloc probability
- Land value: central location, high-demand area increases developer interest
- Plot ratio gap: check URA Master Plan; if building is under-utilised vs allowable plot ratio, redevelopment likely
- Owner composition: young owners more likely to agree than elderly (timing matters)

**Advantages:**
- High exit value: 20–30% premium over individual sale
- Certainty of outcome: all owners exit at same price, no negotiation gap
- Simplified process: collective agreement handles coordination
- May enable desired transition: high payout enables major lifestyle change (overseas move, retirement acceleration)

**Risks:**
- Cannot be guaranteed: depends on owner agreement, developer interest, market conditions
- Long timeline: can take 2–5 years from agreement to completion (you're in limbo)
- No guarantee of hitting premium: if market crashes mid-process, developer may cancel
- Displaced temporarily: may need to move out during redevelopment approval process
- Cannot be forced: even if 80% agree, you cannot opt out and stay (cannot remain in demolished building)

**Retirement impact:**
- Timing dependent: en-bloc can happen during retirement, or not at all (don't rely on it)
- If it happens: major windfall, enabling significant lifestyle upgrade or liquid reserve build
- If it doesn't happen: you maintain property as is; no forced exit

**Cannot plan for en-bloc:** it's a lucky outcome, not a strategy. However, you can monitor probability factors (building age, land value, plot ratio gap) and be ready if opportunity arises.

### Exit Option 6: HDB Lease Buyback Scheme (HDB Specific)

**What it is:**
HDB Lease Buyback Scheme (LBS) allows owners aged 65+ to sell remaining lease back to HDB while retaining right to stay.

**Eligibility:**
- Owner age 65+
- HDB 4-room or smaller (1-room, 2-room, 3-room, 4-room only; 5-room excluded)
- Mortgage fully paid
- Owned for 10+ years

**Mechanics:**
- You sell remaining lease to HDB (e.g., 65-year-old with 71-year lease sells 71-year lease)
- HDB pays you lump sum: typically $45K–$120K depending on remaining lease, location, year
- You retain right to stay in flat (rent-free for life, or pay nominal rent post-65)
- After death, flat reverts to HDB

**Example:**
- Age 67, own 4-room HDB with 72-year lease
- LBS payout: $80K (HDB standard value for this lease length, location)
- CPF top-up from HDB: ~$15K (allocated to RA if you've reached RS)
- Proceed with: $80K cash + $15K CPF top-up
- Continue living in flat: no ongoing housing cost, utilities and maintenance your responsibility

**Advantages:**
- Monetises lease without losing home: keep property, get cash
- Simple process: HDB-managed, clear valuation, no negotiation
- Lump-sum payment: ~$80K–$120K available immediately
- CPF top-up: extra allocation to retirement account
- No moving required: stay in familiar home
- Predictable: HDB offers are standard, not market-dependent

**Risks:**
- Limited payout: $80K–$120K is modest compared to $500K–$1M for private property downsize
- Lease buyback eligibility limited: only 4-room or smaller HDB
- Lease valuation: HDB values leases strictly; if market has appreciated faster than HDB formula, you're undercompensated
- Post-buyback: if you want to sell later (for move overseas), property is essentially unsaleable (no lease remaining; HDB owns it)
- Estate planning: cannot leave HDB to children; flat reverts to HDB after death

**Retirement impact:**
- Housing: secured, no ongoing cost, no maintenance obligation (HDB handles structure)
- Liquidity: $80K–$120K cash release (modest, but helpful)
- Simplicity: clear, no ongoing property management
- Legacy: limited (flat cannot be passed to heirs); but frees children from property burden

**Use case:**
- Suitable if you're in 4-room HDB, aged 65+, want modest cash release without moving
- Not suitable if you own 5-room HDB (ineligible), or private property (LBS not available)
- Ideal if you're comfortable staying in HDB, don't expect inheritance windfall, and want retirement simplicity

### Exit Option 7: Stay Put (If Fully Paid)

**What it is:**
Keep property, make no exit. Live in it, maintain it, accept no liquidity.

**When it works:**
- You're in a paid-off property in excellent condition
- You're deeply attached to the home (family memories, community, location)
- You have sufficient pension/CPF LIFE income that liquidity is not urgent
- You have an estate plan (children will inherit property)
- Property has 50+ years remaining lease (no decay risk)

**Advantages:**
- No moving hassle: stay familiar, low stress in retirement
- No transaction costs: no agent fees, legal fees, BSD/ABSD
- Zero housing cost: fully paid, no mortgage, no ongoing payments
- Community continuity: stay in established neighbourhood, maintain friendships
- Simplicity: one less thing to manage (no property sale/purchase stress)

**Risks:**
- No liquidity: cannot access property value without selling or mortgaging
- Maintenance liability: aging building may require expensive repairs (roof, waterproofing, electrical); your responsibility if condo or private
- Lease decay risk: ensure property has 50+ year remaining lease; if <40 years, resale/refinance becomes difficult later
- Isolation risk: if area becomes undesirable (crime, pollution, urban decay), property value may decline; you're stuck
- Inflexibility: if health crisis forces relocation (move closer to medical care, move to senior living), you're locked in property

**Retirement impact:**
- Housing: secure, known, fully paid
- Liquidity: zero (all wealth locked in illiquid property)
- Flexibility: low (moving later is difficult; ageing in place, or forced sale in crisis)
- Suitable if: you have strong pension + CPF LIFE + investment income, and minimal need for liquid reserves

**Execution timeline:**
- Age 60–65: ensure property is in excellent repair; address deferred maintenance
- Age 65+: settle in for long-term living; enjoy reduced maintenance through condo facilities or family support
- Age 75+: reassess if health changes or major repairs needed (may trigger forced exit then)

## Lease Decay: The Critical Retirement Constraint

Lease decay is the most under-appreciated retirement risk for property owners. Here's why:

### How Lease Decay Works

A 99-year lease purchased at age 30 = lease expires at age 129. No issue.

A 70-year lease purchased at age 50 = lease expires at age 120. Still OK.

A 60-year lease purchased at age 60 = lease expires at age 120. Also OK.

But below 60 years remaining, financing becomes restricted:
- Banks will not lend on leases <30 years
- Banks have stricter lending on leases 30–50 years (higher risk)
- Buyers' pool shrinks: investors won't buy short-lease; owner-occupiers hesitant

### Lease Decay Timeline

| Remaining Lease at Retirement (Age 65) | Property Age at 65 | Concern Level | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 70+ years | 99-year lease purchased at 34 or younger | Low | No action; normal market |
| 60–69 years | 99-year lease purchased at 35–44 | Moderate | Monitor; plan exit within 10 years |
| 50–59 years | 99-year lease purchased at 45–54 | High | Plan exit within 5 years |
| 40–49 years | 99-year lease purchased at 55–64 | Critical | Exit immediately or accept illiquidity |
| <40 years | 99-year lease purchased at 65+ | Unsaleable | Property is essentially locked; cannot finance, cannot sell |

### The $1M Property Becomes $600K When Lease Decays

Example: Property worth $1M with 60-year lease

Compare to identical property with 80-year lease:
- $1M property, 60 years: sells for $700K (30% discount due to financing risk)
- $1M equivalent property, 80 years: sells for $1M (normal market price)
- **Lease decay cost: $300K** on a $1M property, purely from lease expiry

### Retirement Planning for Lease Decay

**At age 60, calculate:**
- Current property lease remaining: _____ years
- Age at lease expiry: 60 + _____ = _____ (this is when property becomes unsaleable)
- Years until critical lease level (40 years): _____ (age when you must exit)

**Example:**
- Age 60, property has 74-year lease remaining
- Lease expires at age 134 (not a concern)
- Critical level (40 years) is at age 94 (no concern)
- Action: can hold property into retirement, no lease decay risk

**Counter-example:**
- Age 60, property has 50-year lease remaining
- Lease expires at age 110 (OK)
- Critical level (40 years) is at age 80 (need to exit by 80)
- Action: must plan exit strategy by age 75–80; don't hold beyond 80

**Don't let lease decay surprise you:**
- Map this at age 50, age 55, age 60
- If critical exit age is before age 80, plan downsize/sale by age 70–75
- Don't hold short-lease property into very old age (90+); it becomes unsaleable

## Decision Framework: Which Exit to Choose?

Use this decision tree:

```
1. Do you own property fully paid off?
   YES → Go to Q2
   NO → Refinance or pay off before considering exits

2. What is remaining lease?
   <40 years → Must exit (property is decaying; sell/downsize)
   40-50 years → Plan exit within 5 years
   50-70 years → Plan exit within 10 years
   70+ years → No urgency; can hold or exit

3. What is your primary goal?
   A. Maximize liquidity → Sell and rent (Option 2) or downsize (Option 1)
   B. Generate income → Monetise via rental (Option 4)
   C. Simplify lifestyle → Right-size (Option 3) or downsize (Option 1)
   D. Preserve capital + legacy → Stay put (Option 7) or monetise (Option 4)

4. What is your health & mobility situation?
   Excellent / young → Can execute complex strategy (rental, en-bloc)
   Moderate → Prefer simpler strategy (downsize, sell-and-rent)
   Declining → Must prioritize simplicity (downsize now, before health limits options)

5. Do you have alternative housing secured?
   YES → Can monetise rental (Option 4)
   NO → Must downsize, sell-and-rent, or right-size (Options 1, 2, 3)

6. What is your family situation?
   No children / children independent → Freedom to downsize, rent, or sell
   Young children → Preserve housing stability (right-size, stay put)
   Adult children willing to inherit → Can monetise rental or stay put
   Adult children want liquidity → Downsize or sell
```

### Example Decisions

**Scenario 1: Age 65, $2.5M fully paid condo, excellent health, independent adult children, passive income sufficient**
- Remaining lease: 85 years (no decay risk)
- Goal: maximize income + flexibility
- **Decision: Monetise via rental**
  - Rent out for $4K/month = $40K/year
  - Rent small apartment = $1.5K/month = $18K/year
  - Net income: $22K/year
  - Rationale: fully paid, great yield, health is good, can manage tenants

**Scenario 2: Age 62, $1.5M HDB 4-room, excellent health, modest CPF LIFE income, grandchildren visit frequently**
- Remaining lease: 95 years (no decay risk)
- Goal: simplify, generate modest income, maintain guest space
- **Decision: Lease Buyback (HDS LBS)**
  - Monetise remaining lease: $80K–$100K
  - Continue living rent-free
  - Rationale: LBS gives cash without moving; HDB handles maintenance; can host grandchildren

**Scenario 3: Age 70, $2M property with 52-year lease, health declining, adult children struggling financially**
- Remaining lease: 52 years (decay risk mounting; must exit by 80)
- Goal: downsize, build liquid reserves, simplify
- **Decision: Sell and downsize**
  - Sell $2M property
  - Buy $1M compact HDB or small condo (ensure 50+ year lease)
  - Release $900K (net after costs)
  - Rationale: exit before lease decay accelerates, build reserves, simplify; health declining so moving now before health crisis forces emergency sale

**Scenario 4: Age 68, $3M condo, excellent health, very healthy CPF LIFE, travel goals**
- Remaining lease: 80 years (no decay risk)
- Goal: maximize flexibility, relocate capability
- **Decision: Sell and rent**
  - Sell $3M property
  - Invest proceeds at 4% = $120K/year
  - Rent flexible apartment
  - Rationale: maximum flexibility for travel/relocation; investment income covers rent + extra

## Execution Checklist: Planning Your Exit

### 1–3 Years Before Planned Exit (Age 55–60 for typical age-65 exit)

- [ ] Calculate remaining lease expiry date
- [ ] Assess property condition (inspection for needed repairs)
- [ ] Research target properties (if downsizing) or target rental costs (if sell-and-rent)
- [ ] Model financial scenarios: how much cash released? What lifestyle is sustainable?
- [ ] Discuss with family: move plan, emotional considerations, legacy intentions
- [ ] Review CPF balance: ensure CPF refund and accrued interest calculated
- [ ] Check investment portfolio: if buying new property, do you have downpayment saved?

### 6–12 Months Before Exit

- [ ] List property for sale (if exiting) or preparation for rental (if monetising)
- [ ] Renovate/refresh property to maximize sale price
- [ ] Secure pre-approval for new property mortgage (if downsizing to purchased property)
- [ ] Identify target rental property or target rental apartment (if downsizing or sell-and-rent)
- [ ] Brief family and prepare for move

### 3–6 Months Before Exit

- [ ] Finalise buyer or rental tenant
- [ ] Coordinate legal process (sale document review, stamping)
- [ ] Arrange moving logistics
- [ ] Notify utilities, change address, update financial institutions
- [ ] Plan handover timing (sale) or transition (rental)

### At Exit

- [ ] Receive proceeds, refund CPF (if used), settle remaining mortgage (if any)
- [ ] Complete purchase or secure rental lease
- [ ] Move in, update address with all institutions
- [ ] Update will and estate plan (if property is transferred / no longer primary asset)

### Post-Exit (First Year)

- [ ] Monitor new living situation: adjusting well? Unexpected costs?
- [ ] Review liquid reserves: build to 1–2 years of expenses if below target
- [ ] Adjust investment strategy: shift to income-generating if needed
- [ ] Plan next phase: if renting, reassess in 2–3 years (rent inflation, opportunity to re-enter market)
- [ ] Review CPF drawdowns and CPF LIFE election (if approaching 65)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Choose your exit based on three factors: lease decay risk, lifestyle goals, and family situation.** Each exit (downsize, rent, right-size, monetise, en-bloc, LBS, stay-put) is valid in different contexts.

2. **Never let lease decay trap you.** Map when your property's lease hits critical levels (40–50 years remaining); plan exit 5–10 years before. Don't hold a short-lease property into very old age; it becomes unsaleable.

3. **Downsize and liquidate is the most common and accessible exit path.** It releases meaningful capital ($500K–$1.5M in typical cases) and is psychologically simpler than selling entirely to rent.

4. **Sell-and-rent maximizes liquidity and flexibility but requires comfort with renting in retirement.** It works best when property value is high relative to rental costs (Singapore's situation post-2020), and when you want to relocate.

5. **Monetise via rental is excellent if property is fully paid and you have alternative housing.** $30K–$50K annual rental income is material in retirement, but only if you're willing to be a landlord at age 65+.

6. **Each exit has a 5–10 year planning window.** Don't wait until age 64 to decide; start mapping at age 55. Health, market conditions, and life circumstances change; starting early gives you options.

7. **Transaction costs are real:** 5–7% on sale, 1.5–4% on purchase, legal fees, renovation. Factor these into any return projections. A property with 50% capital gain can net only 40% after costs.
